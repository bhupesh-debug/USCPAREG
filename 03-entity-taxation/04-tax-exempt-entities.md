# 04 — Tax-Exempt Entities

## What Are Tax-Exempt Organizations?

Tax-exempt organizations are entities that have been granted an exemption from federal income tax because they serve a qualifying purpose. The most common type is the **Section 501(c)(3)** organization.

✅ A tax-exempt organization is like a nonprofit club that the government has agreed not to tax — because the club does something good for society (charity, education, religion, etc.). But the club still has to follow strict rules. If it does things that aren't related to its mission, it can owe tax on those activities.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "PURE" — Tax-Exempt Organization Rules

> **P**urpose must be exempt (charitable, educational, religious, scientific, etc.)  
> **U**nrelated business income is taxed (UBTI)  
> **R**estrictions on political activity (no campaigning)  
> **E**xcess benefits are penalized (private inurement prohibited)  

---

## Section 501(c)(3) Requirements

To qualify as a 501(c)(3) organization, the entity must:

| Requirement | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| **Organized** | Created for an exempt purpose (articles of incorporation must limit purposes) |
| **Operated** | Substantially all activities must further exempt purposes |
| **Exempt purpose** | Charitable, educational, religious, scientific, literary, testing for public safety, amateur sports, prevention of cruelty to children/animals |
| **No private inurement** | Net earnings cannot inure to the benefit of any private individual or shareholder |
| **No substantial lobbying** | Cannot engage in substantial legislative lobbying activity (some allowed) |
| **No political campaign activity** | Absolutely prohibited from participating in political campaigns for or against candidates |

---

## Types of 501(c)(3) Organizations

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Public charity** | Receives broad public support; churches, hospitals, schools, community foundations |
| **Private foundation** | Typically funded by one source (family/corporation); more restrictions apply |

---

## Public Charity vs. Private Foundation

| Feature | Public Charity | Private Foundation |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Funding source | Broadly from public | Narrow — few donors |
| Deductibility for donors (cash) | 60% AGI limit | 30% AGI limit |
| Deductibility for donors (capital gain property) | 30% AGI limit | 20% AGI limit |
| Excise taxes | None generally | 1.39% excise on net investment income |
| Distribution requirements | None | Must distribute 5% of assets annually |
| Prohibited transactions rules | Less strict | Strict (Section 4941–4945) |
| Self-dealing rules | Less strict | Very strict |
| Disclosure | Form 990 | Form 990-PF |

---

## Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI)

Even tax-exempt organizations must pay tax on **UBTI** — income from activities that are:
1. A **trade or business**
2. **Regularly carried on**
3. **Not substantially related** to the exempt purpose

✅ An exempt organization can sell cookies to raise money for its charitable mission — that's related to its purpose. But if it opens a full commercial bakery unrelated to its mission just to make money, that bakery income is taxed. The UBTI rules prevent exempt orgs from competing unfairly with for-profit businesses.

**Tax rate on UBTI:** Taxed at regular corporate rates (21%)

### Excluded from UBTI (NOT taxable)

| Exclusion | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| **Dividends and interest** | Generally excluded (passive investment income) |
| **Royalties** | Generally excluded |
| **Rent from real property** | Excluded unless debt-financed |
| **Gains from property sales** | Excluded (unless inventory or dealer property) |
| **Volunteer labor activities** | If substantially all work done by volunteers |
| **Convenience activities** | Services performed for members/students/employees |
| **Thrift store income** | If substantially all merchandise donated |

### Examples of UBTI

- Advertising income in exempt organization's publications
- Income from operating a parking lot for the general public
- Income from debt-financed rental property (the debt-financed portion)
- Income from certain research conducted for commercial purposes

## Filing Requirements

| Form | Who Files | Due Date |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Form 990** | Most 501(c)(3) organizations (gross receipts ≥ $200,000 OR total assets ≥ $500,000) | 15th day of 5th month after year-end |
| **Form 990-EZ** | Smaller organizations (gross receipts < $200,000 AND assets < $500,000) | Same |
| **Form 990-N (e-Postcard)** | Very small orgs (gross receipts ≤ $50,000) | Same |
| **Form 990-PF** | Private foundations (all, regardless of size) | Same |
| **Form 990-T** | Any exempt org with UBTI | 15th day of 4th month (C corp) |

**Churches** are generally exempt from filing Form 990.

**Failure to file for 3 consecutive years** results in **automatic revocation** of exempt status.

---

## Prohibited Transactions for Private Foundations (Section 4941)

Private foundations face strict rules against **self-dealing** between the foundation and its "disqualified persons":

**Disqualified persons include:**
- Foundation managers
- Substantial contributors
- Family members of above
- Corporations/partnerships/trusts owned 35%+ by above

**Prohibited self-dealing acts:**
- Sale, lease, or exchange of property between foundation and disqualified person
- Loans or credit from foundation to disqualified person
- Payment of compensation by foundation to disqualified person (unless reasonable and for personal services)
- Foundation use of income/assets to benefit disqualified person

**Penalty:** 10% initial excise tax on the amount of self-dealing; 200% if not corrected.

---

## Excess Benefit Transactions (Section 4958)

For **public charities** (not foundations), excess benefit transactions are penalized:

**Excess benefit transaction:** An organization provides an economic benefit to a **disqualified person** that exceeds the value of services/property received in return (e.g., excessive compensation).

**Penalties:**
- **25% excise tax** on the excess benefit amount (on the disqualified person)
- **200%** if not corrected timely
- **10% on organization managers** who approved it knowingly, up to $20,000

✅ This is the rule that says: "You can't pay your charity's executive $10 million in salary just because you're on the board." If you do, the executive pays a 25% penalty on the excess, and the board members who approved it each face a 10% tax.

---

## Political Activity Restrictions

| Activity | Public Charity (501c3) | Social Welfare (501c4) |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Political campaign contributions | **Absolutely prohibited** | Allowed if not primary purpose |
| Endorsing/opposing candidates | **Absolutely prohibited** | Allowed if not primary purpose |
| Voter education (neutral) | Permitted | Permitted |
| Lobbying for legislation | Limited (not substantial) | Permitted |
| Issue advocacy | Permitted | Permitted |

✅ A 501(c)(3) charity cannot say "Vote for Candidate X" or "Don't vote for Candidate Y" — ever. If it does, it risks losing its tax-exempt status. Churches are often reminded of this rule, called the "Johnson Amendment."

## Application for Exemption

| Form | Used By |
|------|---------|
| **Form 1023** | Most 501(c)(3) organizations |
| **Form 1023-EZ** | Small 501(c)(3) organizations (gross receipts ≤ $50K projected) |
| **Form 1024** | Other 501(c) organizations |

Organizations with gross receipts ≤ $5,000 annually are technically exempt without applying, but should still file to receive IRS recognition.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: UBTI — "Three T's Test"

> **T**rade or business (regular commercial activity)  
> **T**ypically/Regularly carried on  
> **T**otally unrelated to exempt purpose  
> All three must be met for income to be UBTI.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Private Foundation Penalties — "10-200 Rule"

> **10%** initial tax for self-dealing  
> **200%** penalty if not corrected in time  
> Better to correct fast — the penalty multiplies if you delay!

---

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** The absolute prohibition on **political campaign activity** applies ONLY to 501(c)(3) organizations. A 501(c)(4) social welfare org can engage in some political activity if it's not the primary purpose.

> **Tip 2:** **UBTI does not include** passive income (dividends, interest, rent from real property, royalties). These are excluded even if the organization earns them from a large portfolio.

> **Tip 3:** Failure to file Form 990 for **3 consecutive years** results in automatic loss of exemption — not a penalty, automatic revocation. This is a common exam trap.

> **Tip 4:** The **5% minimum distribution requirement** applies to **private foundations** only (must distribute 5% of net assets for charitable purposes each year). Public charities have no such requirement.

> **Tip 5:** Public charity donors can deduct cash gifts up to **60% of AGI**; private foundation donors are limited to **30% of AGI**. Capital gain property: 30% public, 20% private.

> **Tip 6:** Excess benefit transactions (Section 4958) apply to **public charities**. Self-dealing rules (Section 4941) apply to **private foundations**. Know which penalty regime applies to which type.
