# 03 — Partnerships

## What Is a Partnership?

A **partnership** is an arrangement between two or more persons to carry on a trade or business as co-owners for profit. Like S corporations, partnerships are **pass-through entities** — the partnership itself pays no federal income tax. Income, losses, deductions, and credits flow through to the partners and are reported on their individual returns.

✅ A partnership is like a team project at school — the team (partnership) does the work, but each person on the team reports their own share of the grade (income/loss) on their own report card (tax return). The team itself doesn't get its own grade from the teacher.

---

## Formation — Section 721

**Rule:** No gain or loss is recognized by the **partnership or partners** when property is contributed to a partnership in exchange for a partnership interest.

**Exceptions (gain IS recognized):**
- Contribution of property where liabilities assumed exceed the adjusted basis of all property contributed (net liability relief)
- Contribution of property in exchange for a partnership interest that is in substance a sale
- Investment company contributions where diversification occurs

**Partner's initial outside basis** = adjusted basis of property contributed + any gain recognized

---

## Inside Basis vs. Outside Basis

| Term | Defined As | Who Has It |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Inside basis** | Partnership's adjusted basis in its own assets | The partnership entity |
| **Outside basis** | Partner's adjusted basis in their partnership interest | Each individual partner |

✅ Inside basis is what the partnership thinks it owns. Outside basis is what each partner thinks their investment is worth for tax purposes. They can differ — especially after special allocations, distributions, or when property is contributed with built-in gain/loss.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "PAID" — Partner's Outside Basis

> **P**lus: cash contributions + basis of property contributed + income/gain + share of partnership liabilities  
> **A**gainst (decrease): distributions received  
> **I**ncludes: the partner's **share of partnership liabilities** (key difference from S corps!)  
> **D**educt: losses and separately stated deductions  

**Formula:**
```
Initial contribution (cash + adjusted basis of property)
+ Share of partnership liabilities (all three types)
+ Distributive share of income and gain items
+ Additional contributions
− Distributions (cash + adjusted basis of property distributed)
− Share of partnership losses
− Partner's share of liabilities that decreased
= Outside Basis (floor: zero)
```

---

## Partnership Liabilities and Basis

Partners include their **share of partnership liabilities** in their outside basis. This is a major difference from S corporations.

| Liability Type | Who Gets Basis |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Recourse liabilities** | Only the partner(s) who bears economic risk of loss |
| **Nonrecourse liabilities** | Allocated based on profit-sharing ratio (generally) |
| **Qualified nonrecourse financing** | Allocated based on profit-sharing ratio |

✅ Because partners get basis for their share of partnership debt, a partner can have more basis than they actually invested with cash. This means they can deduct more losses — a key tax advantage of partnerships over S corps.

---

## Guaranteed Payments

**Guaranteed payments** are amounts paid to a partner for services or use of capital, **without regard to partnership income**. They are:
- Treated as **ordinary income** to the receiving partner
- Deductible by the partnership
- Reported on Schedule K-1

✅ A guaranteed payment is like a salary paid to a partner — even if the partnership loses money, the partner still gets this fixed payment and must report it as income.

---

## Distributive Share

Each partner's **distributive share** of partnership income, gain, loss, deduction, and credit is determined by the **partnership agreement**. If there is no special allocation, it defaults to the partner's interest in the partnership.

**Separately stated items** (must be passed through as-is to preserve their character):
- Capital gains and losses
- Section 1231 gains and losses
- Charitable contributions
- Tax-exempt income
- Investment interest expense
- Credits (investment tax credit, etc.)
- Foreign taxes

---

## Special Allocations

Partners can agree to allocate specific items differently from general profit/loss ratios, BUT the allocation must have **substantial economic effect**:

### Two-Part Test for Substantial Economic Effect

**Economic Effect** (must meet all three):
1. Capital accounts properly maintained
2. Distributions on liquidation follow positive capital account balances
3. Partners with deficit capital accounts must restore the deficit

**Substantiality:** The allocation must not merely shift tax consequences among partners without changing the economic outcome in a meaningful way.

---

## Distributions from Partnerships

### Current (Nonliquidating) Distributions

| Item | Rule |
|------|------|
| Cash distribution | Reduces outside basis; taxable only if exceeds outside basis (excess = capital gain) |
| Property distribution | Takes a basis equal to partnership's inside basis (carry-over), but cannot exceed partner's outside basis |
| Basis reduction order | First reduce cash, then unrealized receivables, then inventory, then other property |

### Liquidating Distributions

When a partner's entire interest is terminated:
- If cash received > outside basis → **capital gain**
- If cash received < outside basis → **capital loss** (only if distribution consists solely of cash, unrealized receivables, or inventory)
- Property received gets an **adjusted basis** to make the books balance (special rules)

---

## Hot Assets — Section 751

**"Hot assets"** = unrealized receivables and substantially appreciated inventory.

**Rule:** When a partner sells their partnership interest or receives a distribution that involves hot assets, the portion attributable to hot assets is treated as **ordinary income** (not capital gain).

✅ Hot assets are called "hot" because they generate ordinary income — not the favorable capital gain treatment you'd normally get on a partnership interest sale. Congress doesn't want partners to convert ordinary income into capital gains by selling a partnership interest.

**Unrealized receivables:** Include rights to receive income that hasn't been earned yet (e.g., cash-method accounts receivable) and potential Section 1245/1250 recapture.

**Substantially appreciated inventory:** Inventory with FMV > 120% of adjusted basis.

---

## At-Risk Rules (Section 465)

**Rule:** Losses deductible only to the extent the partner is "at risk" — meaning they could actually lose that amount.

**At-risk amount includes:**
- Cash and adjusted basis of property contributed
- Recourse debt (personally liable)
- Qualified nonrecourse financing (for real estate)

**NOT at risk:**
- Nonrecourse debt (except qualified nonrecourse financing in real estate)
- Borrowed amounts protected against loss

Losses in excess of at-risk basis are **suspended** and carry forward to future years when the at-risk amount increases.

---

## Passive Activity Loss (PAL) Rules (Section 469)

**Rule:** Passive activity losses can only offset **passive activity income**. Excess passive losses are suspended.

**Activities are passive if:**
- The taxpayer does NOT materially participate, OR
- The activity is a rental activity (generally always passive, with exceptions)

**Material participation tests** (meet any one):
- 500+ hours/year
- Substantially all participation in the activity
- 100+ hours AND no one else participates more
- (plus several other tests)

**Special $25,000 rental allowance:** For rental real estate, taxpayers with AGI ≤ $100,000 can deduct up to $25,000 of passive losses against ordinary income. Phase-out: $100,000–$150,000 AGI.

---

## Partnership Termination

Under current law, a partnership terminates when:
- No part of any business is carried on by any of its partners
- There is a **sale or exchange of 50% or more** of the total interest in capital AND profits within a 12-month period

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Hot Assets = "URI" — Ordinary Income Alert

> **U**nrealized receivables  
> **R**ecapture income potential  
> **I**nventory (substantially appreciated)  
> Whenever these "hot assets" are involved in a sale or distribution, ordinary income lurks.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: At-Risk vs. Passive — "At-Risk First, Passive Second"

> Two separate hurdles for loss deductibility:  
> **Hurdle 1:** Is your loss within your at-risk amount? (Section 465)  
> **Hurdle 2:** Is the activity passive? (Section 469)  
> A loss must clear BOTH hurdles to be currently deductible.

---

## Partnership vs. S Corporation Basis Comparison

| Feature | Partnership (Outside Basis) | S Corporation (Stock Basis) |
|---------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| Initial basis | Adjusted basis of contributed property | Cash/FMV of stock paid |
| Share of entity debt | **YES — increases basis** | **NO — no debt basis** |
| Recourse debt allocation | To partner bearing economic risk | N/A |
| Nonrecourse debt | Allocated by profit ratio | N/A |
| Loss absorption | To the extent of outside basis | To the extent of stock + debt basis |

---

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** The biggest difference between partnerships and S corporations for basis purposes: **partners get basis for their share of partnership liabilities; S corp shareholders do not**. This is the most commonly tested distinction.

> **Tip 2:** **Guaranteed payments** are ordinary income to the recipient and are deductible by the partnership — they're treated like wages/interest paid to an outsider.

> **Tip 3:** For **hot assets** (Section 751), when a partner sells their interest, the gain attributable to unrealized receivables and substantially appreciated inventory is ordinary income, not capital gain.

> **Tip 4:** The **at-risk rules** (Section 465) come before the passive activity rules (Section 469). Apply at-risk first, then passive.

> **Tip 5:** **Special allocations** must have substantial economic effect. If an allocation only shifts taxes without real economic substance, the IRS will reallocate based on the partners' actual interests.

> **Tip 6:** A **cash distribution exceeding outside basis** is a capital gain — but this is rare because outside basis usually includes the partner's share of liabilities. Know the order: basis is reduced, then gain is recognized for any excess.
