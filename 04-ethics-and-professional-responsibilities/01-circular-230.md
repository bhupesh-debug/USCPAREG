# 01 — Circular 230

## What Is Circular 230?

**Circular 230** (Treasury Department Circular No. 230) sets the rules for **practicing before the Internal Revenue Service**. It establishes who can practice, what they must do, and what they must not do. It is issued by the Treasury Department's Office of Professional Responsibility (OPR).

✅ Circular 230 is the rulebook for tax professionals who want to represent clients in front of the IRS. It's like a code of conduct that says: "If you want to be allowed to argue with the IRS on your client's behalf, here are the rules you must follow — and here's what happens if you break them."

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "CIRCLE" — Circular 230 Core Duties

> **C**ompetence (must have the knowledge and skills to handle the matter)  
> **I**ntegrity (honest dealings with the IRS and clients)  
> **R**eturn records to the client promptly upon request  
> **C**lient errors must be communicated (advise client to correct)  
> **L**imits on fees (contingent fees generally prohibited)  
> **E**thics in written tax advice (must meet standards)  

---

## Who Can Practice Before the IRS?

"Practicing before the IRS" includes: preparing and filing tax returns, communicating with the IRS on behalf of a taxpayer, and representing taxpayers in IRS proceedings.

| Practitioner | Practice Rights |
|-------------|----------------|
| **Attorneys** | Full unlimited practice rights |
| **CPAs** | Full unlimited practice rights |
| **Enrolled Agents (EAs)** | Full unlimited practice rights |
| **Enrolled Actuaries** | Limited to pension/actuarial matters |
| **Enrolled Retirement Plan Agents** | Limited to retirement plan matters |
| **Registered Tax Return Preparers** | Limited practice (representation only for returns they prepared) |
| **Annual Filing Season Program participants** | Limited representation rights |
| **Unenrolled preparers** | Can only prepare returns — cannot represent clients before IRS |

---

## Due Diligence Requirements

Practitioners must exercise **due diligence** in:

1. **Preparing returns** — must not ignore easily determined facts
2. **Advising on tax positions** — must inquire about relevant facts
3. **Reliance on client-provided information** — may generally rely on client information without verification, but cannot ignore obviously incorrect, inconsistent, or incomplete information

✅ Due diligence doesn't mean you have to audit everything your client tells you. It means: use professional judgment, ask questions when things don't add up, and don't ignore red flags.

---

## Best Practices Under Circular 230

Practitioners should:
- Communicate clearly with clients about the facts and circumstances
- Establish the facts; determine what further inquiry is needed
- Evaluate the reasonableness of assumptions and representations
- Relate the applicable law to the relevant facts
- Arrive at a conclusion supported by the law and facts
- Act fairly and with integrity in practice before the IRS

---

## Written Tax Advice — Standards

Practitioners providing **written tax advice** must:
- Not base advice on unreasonable factual assumptions
- Not base advice on unreasonable legal positions
- Consider all relevant facts
- Relate the law to the facts and provide a balanced analysis
- Not take into account the likelihood that the position won't be audited

**Covered opinions** (the strictest standard) apply to:
- Written advice concerning listed transactions
- Written advice for marketing of tax shelters

---

## Contingent Fees — Generally Prohibited

**General rule:** Practitioners **cannot** charge contingent fees for:
- Preparation of original tax returns
- Tax advice regarding a position taken on a return

**Exceptions — contingent fees ARE allowed for:**
- Services rendered in connection with a claim for refund filed solely in connection with a determination of statutory interest or penalties
- Examinations or proceedings after a return is filed (audit representation)
- Any judicial proceeding (Tax Court, etc.)
- Collection matters before the IRS

✅ A contingent fee means "I get paid only if you win/get a refund." The IRS is worried that this would create an incentive for tax preparers to take aggressive, improper positions on original returns. So it's generally prohibited except for things like audit representation.

---

## Advertising and Solicitation Rules

**Permitted:**
- Advertising services and fees
- Soliciting new clients through general announcements
- Using the term "Certified Public Accountant" or "CPA"

**Prohibited:**
- False or misleading advertising
- Soliciting potential clients through direct mail or other direct means if the person has made clear they do not want to be solicited
- Uninvited in-person solicitation of prospective clients
- Guaranteeing specific tax results
- Using the term "Certified" in ways that suggest IRS certification

---

## Return of Client Records

**Rule:** Upon request, a practitioner must **promptly return** all records of the client that are necessary for the client to comply with their federal tax obligations, **regardless of any fee dispute**.

**What this means:**
- Cannot withhold client records as leverage for unpaid fees
- The client's own documents must be returned even if the practitioner is owed money
- Exception: Practitioner may retain copies and may withhold documents **created by the practitioner** (working papers) if state law permits and the fee dispute is genuine

✅ You can refuse to hand over your own work product (your firm's notes) in a fee dispute. But you must return the client's own records immediately. The client's tax documents are their property, not yours.

---

## Knowledge of Client Errors

**Rule (Section 10.21):** If a practitioner learns that a client has made an error or omission in any return or document filed with the IRS, the practitioner must:

1. **Promptly advise the client** of the error/omission
2. **Advise the client of the consequences** under the Code and regulations of not correcting the error

**Important limitations:**
- The practitioner does **NOT** have to inform the IRS directly (that's up to the client)
- The practitioner CANNOT continue to represent the client if the client refuses to correct the error and the practitioner's continued representation would violate Circular 230

---

## Sanctions for Circular 230 Violations

The Office of Professional Responsibility (OPR) can impose:

| Sanction | Description |
|----------|------------|
| **Reprimand** | Private censure; no public disclosure |
| **Censure** | Public reprimand |
| **Suspension** | Temporary prohibition from practice (specific time period) |
| **Disbarment** | Permanent prohibition from practice |
| **Monetary penalty** | Up to $5,000 per violation (or gross income from the conduct) for individuals; $10,000 per violation for entities |

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Client Records — "It's Their Records, Not Yours"

> Even if the client owes you money:  
> → Return the **client's own documents** immediately  
> → You can keep YOUR work papers  
> → You can send copies and keep copies  
> → You CANNOT hold their records hostage  

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Contingent Fees — "NO for Returns, YES for Audits"

> **NO** contingent fees on original tax returns or advice about return positions  
> **YES** contingent fees for audit representation, court proceedings, refund claims for interest/penalties  
> The rule is: don't let the fee structure push you toward aggressive original positions.

## Conflicts of Interest (Section 10.29)

A conflict of interest exists when representing one client **directly adverse** to another client, or when representation is **materially limited** by responsibilities to another client, former client, or third party.

**If a conflict exists, the practitioner may still proceed if:**
1. The practitioner reasonably believes they can provide competent representation to all affected clients
2. The representation is not prohibited by law
3. **Each affected client gives informed written consent**

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** The single most tested Circular 230 rule is that a practitioner **must return client records even in a fee dispute**. Memorize this exception to the general principle of withholding for unpaid bills.

> **Tip 2:** Contingent fees on **original returns** are prohibited. Contingent fees on **audit representation** are permitted. The exam will try to blur this line.

> **Tip 3:** When a practitioner discovers a client's prior-year error, the practitioner must **advise the client** — but does NOT have to contact the IRS directly. The decision to correct is the client's.

> **Tip 4:** Practitioners may **rely on client-provided information** without independent verification, unless the information appears obviously incorrect, inconsistent, or incomplete. This is the "reasonable reliance" standard.

> **Tip 5:** Due diligence extends to all aspects of practice — not just return preparation. It applies to advice, representations to the IRS, and all communications.

> **Tip 6:** The OPR can impose sanctions ranging from reprimand to disbarment. Monetary penalties are also available. Know all four levels of sanction.
