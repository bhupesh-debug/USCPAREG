# 03 — Federal Tax Procedures

## Overview

Federal tax procedure covers the IRS's authority to audit taxpayers, collect taxes, and assess penalties — and the taxpayer's rights to challenge these actions.

✅ Tax procedure is like the "rulebook" for the game between the IRS and the taxpayer. Both sides have rights and powers, but they have to follow specific rules, timelines, and procedures. Know the timelines — they are heavily tested.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "3-6-FOREVER" — Statutes of Limitations

> **3 years** — General rule for IRS assessment  
> **6 years** — When > 25% of gross income is omitted  
> **FOREVER** — Fraud or failure to file a return  

---

## IRS Audit Process

### Three Types of IRS Audits

| Type | Description | Location |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Correspondence Audit** | IRS mails a letter requesting documentation for specific items | Done by mail |
| **Office Audit** | Taxpayer comes to an IRS office with records | IRS office |
| **Field Audit** | IRS agent comes to the taxpayer's home or business | Taxpayer's location |

✅ Correspondence audits are the most common — they're simple questions by mail. Field audits are the most serious and comprehensive — the IRS sends an agent to your door.

---

## Statute of Limitations for Assessment

| Situation | Limitation Period | Begins When |
|-----------|-----------------|------------|
| **General rule** | **3 years** | Date return is filed (or due date if filed early) |
| **Substantial omission** — Gross income omitted > 25% | **6 years** | Date return is filed |
| **Fraudulent return** | **Unlimited** | Never expires |
| **No return filed** | **Unlimited** | Never expires |
| **Special circumstances** | IRS and taxpayer can extend by agreement (Form 872) | N/A |

**Important nuance:** Filing early does NOT shorten the period. If return is due April 15 but filed February 1, the 3-year clock starts April 15 (the due date), not February 1. BUT if filed late (after due date), the clock starts on the actual filing date.

✅ The IRS has 3 years to decide to audit you and send you a bill. After that, the case is closed — unless you committed fraud or didn't file at all. Hiding 25%+ of your income gives the IRS 6 years instead. Lying = unlimited time.

---

## Statute of Limitations for Refund Claims

| Situation | Time to File Claim |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **General rule** | **Later of**: 3 years from filing date OR 2 years from tax payment |
| **Claim within 3-year window** | Can claim entire refund for taxes paid in the 3-year lookback |
| **Claim after 3-year window (but within 2 years of payment)** | Only the taxes paid in the last 2 years |

**Form 1040-X** (Amended Individual Return): Must be filed within 3 years of original return due date or 2 years of payment, whichever is later.

## The IRS Appeals Process

```
Taxpayer receives:
  IRS Examination → 30-Day Letter (Preliminary Notice)
           ↓
  Taxpayer can: appeal to IRS Appeals Office within 30 days
           ↓
  If no agreement: 90-Day Letter (Statutory Notice of Deficiency)
           ↓
  Taxpayer has 90 days to:
    Option A: Pay the tax and file a refund claim, then sue in District Court or Court of Federal Claims
    Option B: File a petition with the Tax Court WITHOUT paying first
```

---

## Tax Courts: Choosing a Forum

| Court | Payment Required? | Jury Trial? | Appeals To |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|-----------|
| **U.S. Tax Court** | **No** (can petition before paying) | No | U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals |
| **U.S. District Court** | **Yes** (pay first, then sue for refund) | Yes | U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals |
| **U.S. Court of Federal Claims** | **Yes** (pay first, then sue for refund) | No | U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit |

**Small Tax Case (S Case):** Tax Court cases under $50,000 per year can be handled as an "S Case" — informal, faster, less expensive. BUT decisions cannot be appealed.

✅ The most important distinction: Tax Court lets you **fight without paying** first. If you're cash-strapped and think the IRS is wrong, Tax Court is your friend. District Court lets you have a **jury** — sometimes a strategic advantage.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Courts — "Tax Court = No Pay, District Court = Pay + Jury"

> **Tax Court:** Don't pay, no jury — but you can't appeal S cases  
> **District Court:** Must pay first, but you get a jury trial option  
> **Court of Federal Claims:** Must pay first, no jury, but uniform decisions

## Taxpayer Penalties

### Failure to File Penalty (Section 6651(a)(1))

| Feature | Rule |
|---------|------|
| Rate | **5% per month** (or fraction thereof) |
| Maximum | **25% of unpaid tax** (5 months) |
| Minimum | Greater of $485 (2024) or 100% of tax if return > 60 days late |
| Reasonable cause | Can be waived |

### Failure to Pay Penalty (Section 6651(a)(2))

| Feature | Rule |
|---------|------|
| Rate | **0.5% per month** (or fraction thereof) |
| Maximum | **25% of unpaid tax** (50 months) |
| Reasonable cause | Can be waived |

**Both penalties can run simultaneously**, but when they do:
- The failure-to-file penalty is reduced by the failure-to-pay penalty
- Combined maximum = 5% (4.5% FTF + 0.5% FTP) per month, up to 25% total

✅ The penalty for NOT FILING is 10× worse per month than the penalty for filing but not paying. Always file on time, even if you can't pay. Filing an extension (Form 4868) stops the failure-to-file penalty.

### Accuracy-Related Penalty (Section 6662)

**Rate:** 20% of the underpayment attributable to:
- **Negligence or disregard** of rules and regulations
- **Substantial understatement of income tax** (understatement > greater of 10% of correct tax or $5,000)
- **Substantial valuation misstatement** (value claimed is 150%+ of correct value, underpayment > $5,000)
- **Transfer pricing misstatements**
- **Gross valuation misstatement** (value 200%+ of correct value) → penalty doubles to 40%

**Defense:** Reasonable cause and good faith (can waive penalty)

### Civil Fraud Penalty (Section 6663)

**Rate:** **75% of the underpayment** attributable to fraud

**Elements:**
- Intentional wrongdoing
- Underpayment of tax
- Fraudulent intent to evade taxes

**Burden of proof:** IRS must prove fraud by **clear and convincing evidence**

**Defense:** None effective — fraud is fraud. However, only the fraudulent portion is subject to the 75%; the rest is subject to accuracy-related penalty.

## Innocent Spouse Relief

Three types of relief for spouses who owe taxes because of a joint return error:

| Type | Description | Requirements |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Traditional Innocent Spouse** (§6015(b)) | Relieves spouse of liability for erroneous items of the other spouse | Did not know, had no reason to know of understatement |
| **Separation of Liability** (§6015(c)) | Allocates deficiency between spouses | Must be divorced, legally separated, or lived apart for 12 months |
| **Equitable Relief** (§6015(f)) | Catch-all for cases that don't meet the other tests | Would be inequitable to hold spouse liable; must meet other tests |

✅ If your spouse cheated on the taxes and you didn't know, you can apply for "innocent spouse" relief to avoid being responsible for their errors. But you have to prove you didn't know and had no reason to know.

**Time limit:** Must request relief no later than 2 years after the IRS first attempts to collect the deficiency from the requesting spouse.

## Interest on Underpayments and Overpayments

- **Underpayment interest rate:** Federal short-term rate + 3% (compounded daily)
- **Overpayment interest rate (refunds):** Federal short-term rate + 3% (compounded daily) — same rate
- Interest accrues from the **due date of the return** (not the assessment date)
- Interest is **not waivable** — penalties can be waived for reasonable cause, but interest cannot be

## Amended Returns — Form 1040-X

| Feature | Rule |
|---------|------|
| Form | **Form 1040-X** |
| Time limit | **3 years** from original due date OR **2 years** from payment of tax, whichever is later |
| What can be changed | Any item on the original return |
| IRS authority to audit | Filing amended return does NOT restart the original SOL |
| State returns | Filing a federal amended return usually requires filing a state amended return too |

---

## Burden of Proof

| Situation | Who Bears Burden |
|-----------|----------------|
| **General rule** | **Taxpayer** must prove the IRS assessment is wrong |
| **Burden shift to IRS** | IRS must produce evidence when taxpayer introduces credible evidence and meets record-keeping requirements |
| **Fraud** | **IRS** must prove fraud by clear and convincing evidence |
| **Unreported income (criminal)** | **Government** must prove beyond a reasonable doubt |

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Taxpayer Penalties — "FiFTy, Half, Twenty, SeventyFive"

> **F**ailure to **fi**le = **5%**/month, max **25%** (= fifty)  
> **F**ailure to pay = **0.5%**/month, max **25%** (= **half** of FTF rate)  
> **A**ccuracy-related = **20%** flat  
> **F**raud = **75%** flat

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** The **failure-to-file penalty is 10× the failure-to-pay penalty** per month (5% vs 0.5%). ALWAYS file even if you can't pay — the penalty math strongly favors filing.

> **Tip 2:** The **6-year statute** requires omitting **> 25% of gross income** — not just a small amount. A $100 omission on a $10 million return doesn't trigger the 6-year rule.

> **Tip 3:** Tax Court = **no prepayment required**. District Court = **must pay first**. This is the most-tested court distinction. Remember: the Tax Court is the taxpayer's "pre-payment friend."

> **Tip 4:** Interest cannot be waived for reasonable cause — only **penalties** can be waived. This is a frequent trap: a taxpayer had a good excuse, but interest still accrues.

> **Tip 5:** The **30-day letter** (Preliminary Notice) allows appeal to IRS Appeals. The **90-day letter** (Notice of Deficiency) allows filing in Tax Court. Two different letters, two different timelines.

> **Tip 6:** Innocent spouse relief must be requested within **2 years** of the IRS first attempting collection. Know this deadline — it's easily confused with the 3-year SOL for refunds.
