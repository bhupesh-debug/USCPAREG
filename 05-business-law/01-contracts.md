# 01 — Contracts

## What Is a Contract?

A **contract** is a legally enforceable agreement between two or more parties. It creates mutual obligations that the law will recognize and enforce.

✅ A contract is a promise that the law takes seriously. If you promise to mow my lawn and I promise to pay you $100, we have a contract. If you mow but I don't pay, a court can force me to. If I pay but you don't mow, a court can force you to refund the money or mow.

---

## Contract Formation: The Three Essential Elements

### 1. Offer

An **offer** is a definite proposal by the offeror, communicated to the offeree, creating the power of acceptance.

**Requirements for a valid offer:**
- Must be **definite and certain** (includes subject matter, parties, price, quantity — especially for UCC)
- Must be **communicated** to the offeree
- Must **manifest intent** to be bound (serious)

**NOT an offer:**
- Advertisements (generally invitations to make an offer)
- Price quotes
- Preliminary negotiations
- Statements of opinion or future intent

**When offers terminate:**
- Rejection or counteroffer by offeree
- Lapse of time specified (or reasonable time if none)
- Revocation by offeror (before acceptance)
- Death or incapacity of either party
- Destruction of subject matter
- Illegality

**Irrevocable offers:**
- **Option contract** — offeree pays consideration to keep offer open
- **Firm offer under UCC** — written, signed offer by merchant, irrevocable for stated time (max 3 months) without consideration
- **Detrimental reliance** — offeree reasonably relied on offer to their detriment

### 2. Acceptance

**Acceptance** = unequivocal assent to ALL terms of the offer by someone with the power to accept.

**Rules:**
- Must be by the person to whom the offer was directed
- Must be communicated to the offeror (unless acceptance by performance)
- Must be on the same terms as the offer (mirror image rule — common law)
- Under UCC: additional/different terms may become part of the contract between merchants (battle of the forms)

**Mailbox rule:** Acceptance is **effective when dispatched** (dropped in mailbox, emailed, etc.) — NOT when received. But all other communications (offer, rejection, revocation) are effective when **received**.

### 3. Consideration

**Consideration** = a bargained-for exchange of something of legal value (benefit to promisor OR detriment to promisee).

**Valid consideration:**
- A promise, act, or forbearance
- Must be bargained-for (not past consideration)
- Must have legal value (but courts don't evaluate adequacy)

**NOT valid consideration:**
- Past consideration (doing something you already did)
- Pre-existing duty rule (promising to do what you're already legally obligated to do)
- Moral obligation
- Illusory promises

## Types of Contracts

| Type | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Bilateral** | Both parties exchange promises (most common) |
| **Unilateral** | One party promises in exchange for the other's ACT (not promise) |
| **Express** | Terms stated explicitly (written or oral) |
| **Implied-in-fact** | Terms inferred from conduct |
| **Quasi-contract** | Courts impose a contract to prevent unjust enrichment (no actual agreement) |
| **Executed** | Both parties have fully performed |
| **Executory** | Neither or only one party has fully performed |
| **Valid** | All elements present and enforceable |
| **Void** | No legal effect; cannot be enforced by either party (e.g., illegal contract) |
| **Voidable** | Can be enforced or voided by one party (e.g., contract with a minor) |
| **Unenforceable** | Valid contract that cannot be enforced due to a legal defense (e.g., SOF) |

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "MY LEGS" — Statute of Frauds

> Contracts that MUST be in writing to be enforceable:  
> **M**arriage — promises made in consideration of marriage  
> **Y**ear — contracts that cannot be performed within 1 year from date of making  
> **L**and — contracts for the sale of real property (any interest in land)  
> **E**xecutor — promises to pay estate debts from personal funds  
> **G**oods — UCC sale of goods ≥ $500  
> **S**urety — promise to pay the debt of another (guarantee)  

✅ "MY LEGS" are the six contracts the law requires in writing. Oral contracts CAN be valid, but these six types are unenforceable unless written AND signed by the party to be charged.

**Exceptions to the Statute of Frauds (UCC):**
- Part performance (partial payment received and accepted)
- Specially manufactured goods (can't resell, production begun)
- Admission in court that a contract existed
- Merchant confirmation rule (written confirmation between merchants not objected to within 10 days)

## Contract Defenses

### Fraud

**Elements of Fraud** — all five required:

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "MISRD" — Elements of Fraud

> **M**aterial misrepresentation of fact  
> **I**ntent to deceive (scienter — knew it was false OR reckless disregard)  
> **S**cientific — the party making it knew or should have known  
> **R**easonable reliance by the other party  
> **D**amages suffered  

**Effect:** Contract is **voidable** by the defrauded party (can rescind or affirm)

### Mutual Mistake vs. Unilateral Mistake

| Type | Effect |
|------|--------|
| **Mutual mistake** (both parties mistaken about material fact) | Contract is **voidable** |
| **Unilateral mistake** (only one party mistaken) | Generally **NOT voidable** (tough luck) — UNLESS the other party knew or should have known of the mistake |

### Duress

Physical or economic compulsion that leaves the party no reasonable alternative. Contract is **voidable** by the coerced party.

### Undue Influence

Excessive influence by someone in a position of trust or authority. Contract is **voidable**.

### Illegality

- Contract for illegal purpose = **void** (neither party can enforce)
- Contract partially illegal = courts may sever the illegal portion (divisible contracts)

### Incapacity/Minors

- **Minors** (under 18): Contracts are **voidable** by the minor (not by the adult)
- Exception: Contracts for **necessaries** (food, clothing, shelter) are enforceable against the minor (quasi-contract recovery only)
- **Mental incapacity:** Voidable if adjudicated incompetent; voidable if unable to understand at time of contracting

## Parol Evidence Rule

**Rule:** When parties have a written contract intended as the **final and complete** expression of their agreement (a "fully integrated" contract), evidence of prior or contemporaneous oral agreements that **vary or contradict** the written terms is **inadmissible**.

✅ Once you sign a written contract that is meant to be "the whole deal," you generally can't go to court and say "but we also agreed verbally to X." The rule protects the written contract from being rewritten in court.

**Exceptions — parol evidence IS admissible to:**
- Explain or clarify an ambiguous term
- Show fraud, misrepresentation, duress, mistake (defenses)
- Show a condition precedent to the contract
- Fill gaps in a partially integrated contract
- Show a subsequent modification (after signing)

## Assignment and Delegation

| Concept | Definition | Default Rule |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Assignment** | Transfer of contract rights to a third party (assignee) | Permitted unless contract prohibits or materially changes obligor's duty |
| **Delegation** | Transfer of contract duties to a third party (delegatee) | Permitted unless contract requires personal performance or prohibits |

**Assignment of future wages** — valid in most states. The obligor (payer) must be notified.

**Delegator remains liable** — Delegation doesn't release the original party from liability unless there is a novation (creditor agrees to release original party and accept the delegatee instead).

## Discharge of Contracts

Contracts can be discharged (ended) by:

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Performance** | Both parties fully perform — the most common |
| **Agreement** | Mutual rescission, novation, accord and satisfaction |
| **Impossibility** | Performance becomes objectively impossible (destruction of subject matter, death of personally required party) |
| **Commercial impracticability** (UCC) | Unforeseen, extreme circumstances make performance impractical |
| **Frustration of purpose** | Supervening event destroys the purpose of the contract (rare) |
| **Operation of law** | Bankruptcy, statute of limitations, material alteration of written contract |

---

## Remedies for Breach of Contract

| Remedy | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Compensatory damages** | Puts non-breaching party in position as if contract performed |
| **Consequential damages** | Foreseeable indirect damages resulting from breach (must be foreseeable at time of contract) |
| **Liquidated damages** | Damages agreed upon in advance (valid if: actual damages are difficult to estimate AND amount is reasonable) |
| **Punitive damages** | Generally NOT available for contract breach (reserved for tort law) |
| **Specific performance** | Court orders the breaching party to perform; available only when money damages are inadequate (rare, unique property) |
| **Rescission** | Contract is cancelled; parties restored to original positions |
| **Restitution** | Recovery to prevent unjust enrichment |

## UCC vs. Common Law — Key Differences

| Feature | Common Law (Services, Real Estate) | UCC Article 2 (Goods) |
|---------|-----------------------------------|-----------------------|
| **Mirror image rule** | Applies strictly | Relaxed (battle of the forms) |
| **Consideration modification** | Need consideration | No consideration needed |
| **Firm offers** | Need consideration | Merchant firm offer — no consideration needed, max 3 months |
| **Warranties** | Limited implied warranties | Express + implied warranty of merchantability + fitness for purpose |
| **Perfect tender rule** | N/A | Buyer can reject any non-conforming goods |
| **Statute of Frauds** | Varies | $500+ requires writing |

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** **MY LEGS** — memorize all six Statute of Frauds categories. The exam loves testing which contracts need to be in writing. Watch for "contracts that cannot be performed within 1 year" — if there's any possibility of performance within 1 year, the SOF doesn't apply.

> **Tip 2:** The **mailbox rule** applies to acceptance only — acceptance is effective when sent, not when received. All other communications (offer, revocation, rejection) are effective when received.

> **Tip 3:** A **minor's contract is voidable by the minor** — not void. The adult cannot use minority as a defense. Only the minor can disaffirm.

> **Tip 4:** **Fraud** makes a contract voidable (not void). The defrauded party can choose to enforce the contract or rescind it.

> **Tip 5:** The **parol evidence rule** only bars evidence of prior and contemporaneous agreements. Evidence of a **subsequent modification** is always admissible.

> **Tip 6:** **Liquidated damages clauses** are valid only if they are a reasonable pre-estimate of actual damages, not a penalty. Clauses that are punitive are unenforceable as "penalty clauses."
