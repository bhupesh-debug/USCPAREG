# 02 — Agency

## What Is Agency?

An **agency relationship** exists when one person (the **agent**) is authorized to act on behalf of another person (the **principal**) in dealing with third parties.

✅ Agency is like giving someone a key to act for you. If you give your real estate agent the authority to negotiate a home purchase on your behalf, you (the principal) are legally bound by their acts — as if you negotiated yourself. The agent acts in your name, not their own.

**Three parties involved:**
- **Principal** — the party on whose behalf the agent acts
- **Agent** — the party who acts on behalf of the principal
- **Third party** — the outside party who deals with the agent

---

## Creation of Agency — Types of Authority

### 1. Actual Authority

**Actual authority** = authority that the principal actually grants to the agent, either expressly or by implication.

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Express actual authority** | Explicitly stated, oral or written | "You are authorized to sign contracts up to $10,000" |
| **Implied actual authority** | Reasonably necessary to carry out express authority | Agent authorized to run a store can hire employees |

### 2. Apparent Authority

**Apparent authority** = authority the principal leads third parties to **reasonably believe** the agent has, based on the principal's conduct (not the agent's).

✅ Apparent authority is when the principal accidentally (or intentionally) makes it look like the agent has more power than they really do. If a manager signs contracts for years without objection and then the principal tries to say "she never had authority," the principal is bound — they created the appearance of authority.

**Requirements:**
- Principal's conduct (not just agent's claim) created the appearance
- Third party reasonably relied on that appearance

### 3. Ratification

**Ratification** = the principal, after the fact, approves and adopts an unauthorized act by the agent.

**Requirements for valid ratification:**
1. Agent must have identified themselves as acting for the principal
2. Principal must have full knowledge of all material facts
3. Principal must ratify the **entire** transaction (cannot ratify part and reject the rest)
4. Third party must not have withdrawn before ratification
5. The act must have been capable of authorization at the time it was done

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "LOCAN" — Agent's Duties to Principal

> **L**oyalty — act in the principal's best interest; no self-dealing  
> **O**bedience — follow all lawful instructions of the principal  
> **C**are — exercise reasonable care and skill  
> **A**ccounting — account for all money and property of the principal  
> **N**otification — notify the principal of all relevant information  

---

## Duties of Agent to Principal

| Duty | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Loyalty** | Must act solely for the principal's benefit; cannot have conflicting interests; no self-dealing |
| **Obedience** | Must follow reasonable and lawful instructions; can disobey only if illegal or unethical |
| **Care/Skill** | Must perform with the care and skill that a reasonable person with the agent's expertise would exercise |
| **Accounting** | Must keep principal's property and funds separate from own; account for all funds handled |
| **Notification** | Must promptly notify principal of material facts relevant to the agency |

**Key principle:** Information known by the agent is deemed to be **known by the principal** (imputed knowledge). This can create liability for the principal even when they didn't personally know.

---

## Duties of Principal to Agent

| Duty | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Compensation** | Pay the agent the agreed-upon compensation (or reasonable value if not specified) |
| **Reimbursement** | Reimburse the agent for authorized expenses incurred |
| **Indemnification** | Hold the agent harmless for losses incurred through authorized acts |
| **Cooperation** | Cannot interfere with the agent's ability to perform |

✅ The relationship runs both ways — the principal isn't just giving orders. They have to pay the agent, cover their costs, and not throw them under the bus for doing what they were told.

---

## Liability of Principal for Agent's Contracts

| Situation | Principal Liability |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Authorized acts (actual authority) | **Fully liable** |
| Apparent authority | **Liable** (principal created the appearance) |
| Ratification | **Liable** (chose to adopt the act) |
| Unauthorized acts with NO authority | **Not liable** (unless ratified) |

---

## Disclosed vs. Undisclosed Principal

| Type | Third Party Knows? | Who is Liable to Third Party? |
|------|------------------|------------------------------|
| **Disclosed principal** | Yes, knows principal's identity | **Principal** is liable; agent is not (typically) |
| **Partially disclosed principal** | Knows agent acts for someone, but not who | Both **principal AND agent** may be liable |
| **Undisclosed principal** | No, thinks agent is acting for themselves | **Both agent AND principal** are liable; third party can elect either |

✅ If you don't reveal that you're working for someone else, you (the agent) can be held personally responsible for the deal. Once the principal is disclosed, you're off the hook. That's the incentive to reveal your principal.

---

## Respondeat Superior — Employer Liability

**Rule:** An employer (principal) is **vicariously liable** for torts committed by an employee (agent) if the tort was committed within the **scope of employment**.

**Within scope of employment:**
- Authorized acts and those incidentally connected to authorized acts
- Acts motivated at least in part by a purpose to serve the employer
- Foreseeable acts given the nature of employment

**Outside scope of employment ("frolic"):**
- Substantial departure from assigned duties (employee on personal errand)
- Employer generally NOT liable

✅ If a delivery driver causes an accident while making deliveries = employer liable. If that same driver takes a detour to visit their friend on personal time and causes an accident = employer NOT liable. The key question: was the employee doing work business?

**Employer is NOT liable for torts of independent contractors** (generally) — unless:
- Inherently dangerous activities
- Non-delegable duties (certain safety obligations)
- Employer was negligent in selecting contractor

---

## Employee vs. Independent Contractor

| Factor | Employee | Independent Contractor |
|--------|---------|----------------------|
| Control over work | Employer controls HOW | Controls own methods |
| Tools/equipment | Employer provides | Own tools |
| Schedule | Set by employer | Sets own hours |
| Multiple clients | Typically no | Yes |
| Tax withholding | Yes (employer withholds) | No (1099) |
| Benefits | Often provided | No |

✅ The main question is: does the company control not just WHAT is done, but HOW it's done? If yes = employee. If the worker controls their methods and brings their own expertise = independent contractor.

## Termination of Agency

### Methods of Termination

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Mutual consent** | Both parties agree to end the relationship |
| **Lapse of time** | Agency was for a fixed period; time expires |
| **Purpose achieved** | Agency was for a specific task; task complete |
| **Revocation by principal** | Principal terminates agent's authority |
| **Renunciation by agent** | Agent terminates the relationship |
| **Operation of law** | Death or incapacity of either principal or agent; bankruptcy; illegality of agency's purpose; destruction of subject matter |

### Effect on Apparent Authority

Even after actual authority ends, **apparent authority may continue** until third parties are notified.

- Parties who **previously dealt with the agent** must receive **actual notice** of termination
- Parties who **knew of the agency** but never dealt must receive **constructive notice** (publication in newspaper)
- Exception: Death or incapacity of principal terminates authority automatically, even without notice

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Types of Authority — "AAR = Agent's Authority Route"

> **A**ctual (Express + Implied) — what the principal really gave  
> **A**pparent — what it looked like the agent had (principal's fault)  
> **R**atification — principal adopts after the fact  
> All three can bind the principal to third-party contracts.

---

## Agency Coupled with an Interest

A special type of agency where the agent has a property or financial interest in the subject matter of the agency.

**Key feature:** The principal **cannot revoke** an agency coupled with an interest (unlike ordinary agency). It is also not terminated by the principal's death.

**Example:** A lender who is authorized to sell collateral if the borrower defaults has an agency coupled with an interest — the lender cannot be fired from that role.

---

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** **Apparent authority** requires the principal to have done something that created the reasonable belief of authority. The agent cannot create their own apparent authority by claiming to have it.

> **Tip 2:** For an **undisclosed principal**, both the agent AND the principal are liable to the third party. The third party gets to choose which one to pursue, but cannot collect double recovery.

> **Tip 3:** **Respondeat superior** applies to employees, not independent contractors. The distinction is degree of control — does the employer control how the work is done?

> **Tip 4:** Information the agent knows is **imputed to the principal** — even if the principal never personally received the information. This is the "imputed knowledge" rule.

> **Tip 5:** **Death of the principal** terminates actual authority automatically, even without notice to the agent. Apparent authority also terminates — third parties are not protected after the principal's death.

> **Tip 6:** An agent always has a duty of **loyalty** — they cannot secretly work for a competitor or take a kickback from a third party. Self-dealing violates the loyalty duty.
