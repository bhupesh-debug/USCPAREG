# 04 — Business Structures

## Overview

Choosing the right business entity affects liability, taxes, management, and the ability to raise capital. The REG exam tests comparisons across all major entity types.

✅ Think of business structures like different types of containers for running a business. Each container has different rules about who's responsible for debts, how profits are taxed, and who's in charge. Choosing the wrong container can cost you money or expose you to personal liability.

---

## The Six Major Business Structures

| Entity | Formation | Liability | Taxation | Management | Transferability |
|--------|-----------|-----------|----------|------------|----------------|
| **Sole Proprietorship** | Automatic (just start) | Unlimited personal | Schedule C (pass-through) | Owner only | Not transferable (not a separate entity) |
| **General Partnership** | Agreement (oral or written); no filing required | Unlimited personal (joint and several) | Form 1065; K-1 pass-through | All partners equal (unless agreement) | Requires consent of all partners |
| **Limited Partnership** | Filed certificate of LP; at least 1 GP + 1 LP | GP: unlimited; LP: limited to investment | Form 1065; K-1 pass-through | GP manages; LP cannot manage without losing limited liability |  LP interests transferable; GP consent required |
| **LLC** | Articles of organization filed with state | Limited to investment | Flexible (disregarded, partnership, or corporate) | Member-managed or manager-managed | Varies by state; operating agreement governs |
| **C Corporation** | Articles of incorporation + bylaws | Limited to investment | Double taxation: 21% corporate + shareholder | Board + officers | Freely transferable (shares) |
| **S Corporation** | Articles of incorporation + Form 2553 election | Limited to investment | Pass-through (Form 1120-S; K-1) | Board + officers | Restricted (max 100 shareholders; eligible shareholders only) |

---

## Detailed Analysis by Entity

### 1. Sole Proprietorship

✅ The simplest business structure — you are the business. No paperwork, no separate entity. Everything you own is at risk if the business gets sued.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Formation** | None required — just start operating |
| **Liability** | Owner personally liable for all business debts (unlimited) |
| **Taxation** | Income reported on Schedule C; subject to SE tax (15.3%) |
| **Management** | Owner has complete control |
| **Capital raising** | Limited to owner's personal resources and loans |
| **Continuity** | Ends with owner's death or decision to stop |
| **Best for** | Single-owner business, low liability risk |

---

### 2. General Partnership (GP)

✅ Two or more people go into business together. No paperwork required to form one — but everyone is fully on the hook for everything the business does, including each other's mistakes.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Formation** | Agreement (can be oral); no state filing required |
| **Liability** | Each partner is **jointly and severally liable** for all partnership debts (creditor can pursue one partner for the full amount) |
| **Taxation** | Pass-through on Form 1065; each partner reports share on Schedule K-1 |
| **Management** | Equal rights unless partnership agreement states otherwise |
| **Partner authority** | Each partner is an agent of the partnership — can bind the partnership |
| **Transferability** | Assignment of economic rights allowed; admission of new partner requires unanimous consent |
| **Continuity** | Technically dissolves upon any partner's death/withdrawal (but most agreements allow continuation) |

**Uniform Partnership Act (UPA):** Governs most GP law where a partnership agreement is silent.

---

### 3. Limited Partnership (LP)

✅ A partnership with two classes of partners: general partners (GP) who run everything and bear unlimited liability, and limited partners (LP) who are passive investors with protection from liability beyond their investment.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Formation** | Must file a **certificate of limited partnership** with the state |
| **At least** | 1 general partner + 1 limited partner |
| **GP liability** | **Unlimited personal liability** |
| **LP liability** | **Limited to their investment** — but ONLY if they don't participate in management |
| **LP management** | Cannot take part in management without risking unlimited liability (safe harbor rules provide exceptions) |
| **Taxation** | Pass-through on Form 1065 |
| **Best for** | Real estate ventures, private equity funds |

---

### 4. Limited Liability Company (LLC)

✅ The LLC is the Swiss Army knife of business structures — it gives you limited liability protection like a corporation, but lets you choose how to be taxed. Most popular choice for small businesses today.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Formation** | File **articles of organization** with the state; operating agreement governs internal affairs |
| **Liability** | All members have **limited liability** (personal assets protected) |
| **Management** | Member-managed (all members participate) OR manager-managed (designated managers run it) |
| **Taxation** | **Flexible** — elect to be taxed as: sole proprietor (single-member), partnership (multi-member), C corp, or S corp |
| **Default taxation** | Single-member = disregarded entity (Schedule C); Multi-member = partnership (Form 1065) |
| **Transferability** | Generally requires consent of other members to admit a new member |
| **Charging order protection** | Creditors of a member typically limited to "charging order" against distributions |
| **Self-employment tax** | Members who work in the business may owe SE tax on their distributive share |

---

### 5. C Corporation

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Formation** | File **articles of incorporation** with the state + bylaws; issue stock |
| **Liability** | Shareholders limited to investment (**corporate veil**) |
| **Taxation** | **Double taxation** — 21% flat corporate rate; shareholders pay again on dividends |
| **Management** | Shareholders elect Board of Directors; Directors appoint Officers |
| **Capital raising** | Can issue unlimited shares of multiple classes; best entity for investment |
| **Transferability** | Shares freely transferable (public company) |
| **Perpetual existence** | Does not dissolve on shareholder death or departure |
| **Fringe benefits** | Full exclusion for all employees (including owner-employees) |
| **Best for** | Large businesses, businesses seeking venture capital or IPO |

---

### 6. S Corporation

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Formation** | Articles of incorporation + **Form 2553 S election** |
| **Liability** | Shareholders have limited liability |
| **Taxation** | **Pass-through** — no corporate tax; income/loss flows to shareholders on K-1 |
| **Restrictions** | ≤ 100 shareholders; US persons/certain trusts only; 1 class of stock; domestic only |
| **SE tax advantage** | Shareholders/employees can take salary (subject to FICA) + distributions (not subject to SE tax) |
| **Best for** | Small-to-medium businesses with few shareholders wanting pass-through treatment |

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Entity Selection — "LIMITS" Checklist

> When choosing an entity, consider:  
> **L**iability — How much personal risk can owners absorb?  
> **I**ncome tax — Pass-through or entity-level? Double tax acceptable?  
> **M**anagement — Who will run the business? How much flexibility?  
> **I**nvestors — Do you need outside investors? IPO in the future?  
> **T**ransferability — Do owners need to freely buy/sell their interests?  
> **S**elf-employment tax — Want to minimize SE tax? (S corp helps)  

---

## Entity Comparison: Liability and Taxation Matrix

| Entity | Personal Liability? | Tax Treatment |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|
| Sole proprietorship | **Yes — unlimited** | Schedule C + SE tax |
| General partnership | **Yes — unlimited, joint & several** | K-1 pass-through; SE tax on active partners |
| Limited partnership | GP: Yes; LP: **No** (if non-managing) | K-1 pass-through |
| LLC | **No** (limited to investment) | Flexible — disregarded, partnership, or corporate |
| C corporation | **No** (limited to investment) | Double tax (21% + dividend tax) |
| S corporation | **No** (limited to investment) | K-1 pass-through; salary + distribution strategy |

---

## Piercing the Corporate Veil

Courts can hold shareholders **personally liable** despite limited liability protection ("pierce the veil") when:
- The corporation is used to **commit fraud**
- The corporation and shareholders are **so intermingled** that the corporation is the alter ego of the shareholders (commingling funds, ignoring formalities)
- Undercapitalization at formation

✅ Limited liability is NOT absolute. If you use your corporation as a personal piggy bank, never hold meetings, mix personal and business money, or use it to defraud people — a court can strip away the liability protection and hold you personally responsible.

---

## Partnership Authority — Joint and Several Liability Detail

In a general partnership:
- **Jointly and severally liable** = a creditor can sue ONE partner for the FULL debt, even if that partner is only 10% owner
- That partner then has **contribution rights** against the other partners
- Incoming partners are NOT personally liable for obligations that arose before they joined

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: Limited Partnership Warning — "LP = Lose Protection if you Participate"

> Limited Partners get limited liability protection ONLY if they stay out of management.  
> **L**imited **P**artners = **L**ose **P**rotection if they **P**articipate in management.  
> Safe harbor activities (voting on major decisions, advising the GP) generally don't destroy LP status.

---

## Entity Selection Factors for the Exam

| Goal | Best Structure |
|------|---------------|
| Simplest setup | Sole proprietorship |
| Limited liability + pass-through tax | LLC or S corp |
| Maximum investors/capital raising | C corporation |
| Real estate with passive investors | Limited partnership or LLC |
| Avoiding SE tax on business profits | S corporation (salary + distribution split) |
| International investors | C corporation (S corp prohibits nonresident aliens) |
| Multiple classes of stock | C corporation (S corp allows only one) |
| Employee stock options (ISOs) | C corporation |

---

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** **General partnership** is the ONLY major structure that requires NO filing with the state. All others require some state filing (certificate, articles, etc.).

> **Tip 2:** In a general partnership, each partner is an agent with authority to bind the partnership in the ordinary course of business — even without express authorization from the others.

> **Tip 3:** An LP loses limited liability if they **participate in management**. The key word is "control" — voting on fundamental decisions generally doesn't destroy LP status, but day-to-day management does.

> **Tip 4:** **LLC is the most flexible** tax-wise — a single-member LLC defaults to disregarded entity (Schedule C), but it can elect S corp or C corp treatment.

> **Tip 5:** The **S corporation SE tax advantage**: the owner-employee takes a "reasonable salary" (subject to FICA) and additional distributions (NOT subject to SE tax). This is a common exam topic and a real planning strategy.

> **Tip 6:** **Piercing the veil** requires either fraud or disregard of corporate formalities (alter ego). Simply being undercapitalized alone is usually not sufficient — courts want evidence of abuse of the corporate form.
