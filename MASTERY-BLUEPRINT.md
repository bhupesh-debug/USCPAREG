## 1. 🎯 The 80/20 Core Insights Framework

✅ **Think of REG like a house.** 80% of the house is held up by just 20% of the beams. Find those beams, and the whole structure makes sense.

The REG exam has 5 content areas. But roughly **20% of the concepts** appear in about **80% of exam questions**. Here are those golden beams:

### The 20% That Controls 80% of Your Score

#### 🏠 Beam 1: Individual Tax Formula (22–32% of exam)

✅ **The "Tax Sandwich" — mnemonic: GI-FART-D**

| Step | What It Means | Memory Trick |
|------|---------------|--------------|
| **G**ross Income | Everything you earn — wages, interest, rent, alimony (pre-2019) | **G**rab all the money |
| **I**ncome adjustments | Above-the-line deductions (student loans, IRA, HSA, SE tax) | **I** adjust my belt |
| **F**iling status | Single, MFJ, MFS, HOH, QSS — determines brackets & standard deduction | **F**igure out who you are |
| **A**GI | Gross Income minus Adjustments = AGI (the most important number) | **A**lways the **G**oal **I**ntermediate |
| **R**educe by deduction | Standard ($14,600 single / $29,200 MFJ) or Itemized (SALT, mortgage, charity, medical>7.5%) | **R**educe the pile |
| **T**axable income | AGI minus deductions = what you actually pay tax on | **T**he final answer |
| **D**etermine tax & credits | Apply brackets, then subtract credits (Child Tax $2,000, EIC, education) | **D**one — credits save the day |

🧠 **Mnemonic: "GI FART-D"** — Gross Income, Filing status, Adjustments, Reduce (deductions), Taxable income, Determine tax

#### 🏠 Beam 2: Entity Taxation Comparison (23–33% of exam)

✅ **Every business is like a different kind of vehicle.** A C-Corp is a bus (passengers separate from the bus), an S-Corp is a carpool (income flows through to each rider), and a Partnership is a tandem bicycle (everyone pedals and shares equally).

| Feature | C-Corp | S-Corp | Partnership |
|---------|--------|--------|-------------|
| Tax Level | Entity pays (21% flat) | Pass-through to shareholders | Pass-through to partners |
| Double Taxation? | YES (corp + dividends) | NO | NO |
| Max Owners | Unlimited | 100 shareholders | Unlimited |
| Owner Types | Anyone | US citizens/residents, no entities (except certain trusts/estates) | Anyone |
| Loss Limitations | Carried forward at corp level | Basis → At-Risk → Passive → Excess Business Loss | Basis → At-Risk → Passive → Excess Business Loss |
| Self-Employment Tax? | No (wages only) | No (reasonable salary required) | General partners: YES |
| Special Form | 1120 | 1120-S + K-1 | 1065 + K-1 |

🧠 **Mnemonic: "BARP" for loss limitation order** — **B**asis → **A**t-**R**isk → **P**assive Activity → Excess Business Loss

🧠 **Mnemonic: S-Corp eligibility = "QRSTUV"**
- **Q**ualified shareholders only (citizens/residents)
- **R**estricted to 100 shareholders
- **S**ingle class of stock
- **T**reated as domestic
- **U**nqualified entities can't own (no partnerships, corps)
- **V**ote differences OK (but not economic)

#### 🏠 Beam 3: Property Transactions — Basis & Characterization (5–15% of exam)

✅ **Basis is like the "receipt" for everything you own.** It's what you paid. When you sell something, your gain = what you got minus your receipt (basis). That's it.

**The Big Formula:**
```
Amount Realized  −  Adjusted Basis  =  Gain or Loss
(what you got)     (what you paid + improvements − depreciation)
```

**Character matters** — is the gain ordinary, capital, or Section 1231?

🧠 **Mnemonic: "HOLD IT" for capital assets (what IS a capital asset)**
- **H**ouse (personal residence)
- **O**utside investments (stocks, bonds)
- **L**uxury items (jewelry, art held personally)
- **D**on't include inventory, depreciable biz property, receivables, real property used in biz, IP created by taxpayer
- **I**t's everything NOT used in your business
- **T**reasure you hold for investment

🧠 **Mnemonic: "I RAID CUP" for what is NOT a capital asset**
- **I**nventory
- **R**eceivables
- **A**ccounts from services
- **I**ntellectual property (self-created)
- **D**epreciable business property
- **C**opyrights (self-created)
- **U**sed supplies
- **P**ublications (government)

#### 🏠 Beam 4: Business Law Essentials (15–25% of exam)

✅ **Contracts are just promises the law will enforce.** Think of a contract like a handshake that a judge will hold you to.

**Three must-know areas:**

**A. Contract Formation = "COAL"**
- **C**onsideration (both sides give something)
- **O**ffer (someone proposes)
- **A**cceptance (someone agrees)
- **L**egal capacity & legality (both parties able, purpose legal)

**B. Agency Law = "The Boss-Worker Rule"**
- Principal (boss) ↔ Agent (worker) ↔ Third Party (customer)
- Agent can bind principal to contracts
- Three types of authority: **Actual** (express/implied), **Apparent** (third party reasonably believes), **Ratification** (boss approves after the fact)

🧠 **Mnemonic: "AAR" — Actual, Apparent, Ratification**

**C. Debtor-Creditor = "Who Gets Paid First?"**
- Secured creditors beat unsecured
- Priority: Secured → Administrative expenses → Domestic support → Tax claims → General unsecured

🧠 **Mnemonic: "SADTU" for bankruptcy priority** — **S**ecured, **A**dmin, **D**omestic support, **T**ax, **U**nsecured

#### 🏠 Beam 5: Ethics & Circular 230 (10–20% of exam)

✅ **Circular 230 is the IRS's rulebook for tax practitioners.** Think of it like the "driver's manual" — you need to know the rules before you're allowed on the road.

**Key rules:**
- Must have **reasonable basis** (>1/3 chance) for non-frivolous positions
- Must have **substantial authority** (>40%) to avoid penalty on undisclosed positions
- Must have **more likely than not** (>50%) for tax shelter positions
- Can NOT charge contingent fees for original returns (only amended or claims)
- Must return client records even if fees unpaid (in most jurisdictions)
- Penalty for willful violation: censure, suspension, disbarment, monetary penalty

🧠 **Mnemonic: "RSM" for position standards (ascending)**
- **R**easonable basis — 1/3 (disclosed non-tax-shelter)
- **S**ubstantial authority — 40% (undisclosed non-tax-shelter)
- **M**ore likely than not — 50% (tax shelters, any)

## 3. 📖 Story-Based Metaphors for Key Concepts

✅ **The best way to remember tax law is to turn it into a movie in your head.** Here are the stories that top 0.1% scorers use:

### Story 1: "The Tax Sandwich Shop" (Individual Tax Formula)

Imagine you own a sandwich shop called **"GI FART-D's Deli."**

1. **Gross Income** = All the money that comes into the shop (sales, tips, catering). You count EVERYTHING.
2. **Adjustments** = Ingredients you remove before building the sandwich (spoiled lettuce, expired cheese). These are "above the line" — they reduce your pile before you even start cooking.
3. **AGI** = The remaining clean ingredients after removing the bad stuff. This is your working pile.
4. **Deductions** = You pick either the House Recipe (Standard Deduction — $14,600 single) or Custom Order (Itemized — SALT, mortgage, charity). Most customers take the House Recipe.
5. **Taxable Income** = The final sandwich. This is what goes on the plate.
6. **Tax & Credits** = The price tag on the sandwich (tax brackets), but you hand out coupons to some customers (credits). Credits reduce the total bill directly.

**The punchline:** Credits are better than deductions because deductions reduce the ingredients (taxable income), but credits reduce the final price (actual tax owed). A $1,000 credit saves exactly $1,000. A $1,000 deduction saves $1,000 × your tax rate.

### Story 2: "Three Brothers Open a Business" (Entity Selection)

Three brothers — Charlie, Sam, and Pete — each open a restaurant.

**Charlie (C-Corporation):**
Charlie hires a manager and makes the restaurant a separate legal person. The restaurant pays its own taxes at 21%. When the restaurant gives Charlie dividends, HE pays tax again on them. Charlie mutters: "I'm being taxed TWICE." But Charlie's restaurant can grow unlimited — anyone can invest.

**Sam (S-Corporation):**
Sam says "No double taxation for me." His restaurant doesn't pay its own tax. Instead, all profits flow directly to Sam's personal return. But Sam has strict rules: max 100 investors, all must be US citizens/residents, only ONE class of stock. Sam also must pay himself a "reasonable salary" (the IRS watches this).

**Pete (Partnership):**
Pete teams up with a friend. They share everything — profits, losses, and self-employment tax (ouch). There's no separate entity tax. It's the simplest setup, but Pete and his partner are personally liable for business debts.

🧠 **Remember: "Charlie pays Corporate tax, Sam Skips it, Pete Passes it through"**

### Story 3: "The Garage Sale" (Basis & Gains)

You bought a bicycle for $500 (your **basis**). You added a fancy seat for $100 (improvement — basis goes UP to $600). You claimed $200 in depreciation over the years (basis goes DOWN to $400). You sell the bike for $700.

```
Amount Realized ($700) − Adjusted Basis ($400) = $300 Gain
```

But what KIND of gain?
- If you held it for fun (personal) → It's a **capital gain** (long-term if held >1 year)
- If it's business equipment → It might be **§1231 gain** (best of both worlds — gains are capital, losses are ordinary)
- The $200 depreciation you claimed? The IRS wants that back → **§1245 depreciation recapture** (taxed as ordinary income)

🧠 **Mnemonic: "The IRS Never Forgets Depreciation" — §1245 recaptures what you deducted**

### Story 4: "The Wedding Planner" (Agency Law)

A bride hires a wedding planner (Agent) to book a venue. The planner signs a contract with a hotel on behalf of the bride (Principal).

- **Actual Authority:** The bride said "Book any venue under $10,000" → the planner books one for $8,000 → VALID.
- **Apparent Authority:** The bride never told the hotel she fired the planner. The planner books another event → the hotel reasonably believed the planner was still authorized → the bride may be BOUND.
- **Ratification:** The planner books a $15,000 venue without permission. The bride finds out and says "Actually, I love it. I'll keep it." → Bride ratifies the unauthorized act.

🧠 **Remember: "The bride is always responsible when the hotel reasonably believes the planner has authority"**

### Story 5: "The Rulebook vs. The Speed Limit" (Circular 230 Standards)

Think of tax positions like driving speeds:

- **Reasonable Basis (1/3 chance):** Going 5 mph over the limit. It's a weak position, but if you disclose it on the return, you're probably fine. Like putting a "STUDENT DRIVER" sign on your car.
- **Substantial Authority (40% chance):** Going exactly the speed limit. This is the default standard for undisclosed positions. Safe enough that you don't need to warn anyone.
- **More Likely Than Not (>50%):** Driving 5 mph UNDER the limit. Required for tax shelters because the stakes are huge. You need to be more right than wrong.

🧠 **"RSM" — Reasonable (1/3), Substantial (40%), More-likely (50%)**

## 4. 🏋️ Training Regimen & Elite Feedback Loops

✅ **Train like a professional athlete — not a student.** The top 0.1% of CPA candidates don't just study; they practice with a system.

### The "3-Phase Practice System"

#### Phase 1: MCQ Drills (70% of practice time)

**Daily MCQ Protocol:**
- Do 30–50 MCQs per day on the topic you studied
- Set a timer: **1.5 minutes per MCQ** max
- After each set, score yourself and sort into:
  - ✅ **GREEN:** Got it right and understood why → Move on
  - 🟡 **YELLOW:** Got it right but guessed → Review the concept
  - 🔴 **RED:** Got it wrong → Write down the rule in your own words, create a flashcard

**The "Wrong Answer Journal":**
Keep a running document (or spreadsheet) of every question you miss:

| # | Date | Topic | Why I Missed It | Rule/Mnemonic |
|---|------|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| 1 | Day 3 | AGI | Forgot student loan interest is above-the-line | "Adjustments = above the line = before the sandwich is built" |
| 2 | Day 5 | S-Corp | Confused basis with at-risk amount | "BARP: Basis → At-Risk → Passive → Excess Business Loss" |

Review this journal every Sunday. Your mistakes are your goldmine.

#### Phase 2: TBS Practice (25% of practice time)

**TBS Training Protocol (Start Week 2):**
- Do 2–3 TBS per study session
- Allot **15 minutes per TBS** (you get about 15 min each on exam day)
- Focus on the top 5 most-tested TBS types:
  1. Individual Tax Return Completion (1040)
  2. Basis Calculation (S-Corp or Partnership)
  3. Entity Classification & Comparison
  4. Property Transaction Gain/Loss
  5. Circular 230 Compliance Scenarios

See the full [TBS Deep-Dive Guide](./resources/task-based-simulations.md) for worked examples.

**TBS Power Move:** Before solving, READ THE ENTIRE TBS first. Identify what data you need and what they're really asking. Then solve systematically — row by row, cell by cell.

#### Phase 3: Full Simulations (5% of practice time — Weeks 3–4 only)

- Take 2–3 full-length practice exams in the final 10 days
- Simulate REAL conditions: 4-hour block, no phone, no notes
- After each exam, analyze mistakes and update your Wrong Answer Journal

### Elite Feedback Loops

**The "Teach Back" Method:**
After studying a topic, explain it out loud to an imaginary student (or a real friend). If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.

**The "48-Hour Recall" Test:**
Two days after studying a topic, close your notes and write everything you remember. Compare with your notes. The gaps reveal what you need to review.

## 5. 📝 Task-Based Simulations — Most-Asked Deep Dive

✅ **TBS are worth 50% of your score.** These are the questions where the exam gives you a scenario and you have to DO something — fill in a tax form, calculate basis, classify transactions.

### The Top 5 Most-Tested TBS (Ranked by Frequency)

#### TBS #1: Individual Tax Return Completion (Form 1040)

**What they give you:** W-2s, 1099s, personal info, receipts for deductions.
**What they ask:** Complete portions of Form 1040 (income, deductions, credits, tax owed).

**Key Steps:**
1. Calculate Gross Income (add all W-2 wages + 1099-INT + 1099-DIV + 1099-MISC)
2. Subtract above-the-line adjustments (student loan interest, IRA, HSA, SE tax deduction)
3. Calculate AGI
4. Choose Standard or Itemized deduction (compare both — pick the higher one)
5. Calculate taxable income
6. Apply tax brackets
7. Subtract credits (Child Tax Credit, Education Credits, EIC)
8. Compare to withholdings → Refund or Amount Owed

**Trap Alert:** Watch for phaseouts. Many credits and deductions phase out at higher AGI levels. The exam loves testing whether you applied the phaseout correctly.

🧠 **Mnemonic: "GRADS CT" — Gross income, Reduce (adjustments), AGI, Deductions, Subtract for Taxable income, Credits, Tax owed**

#### TBS #2: Partnership/S-Corp Basis Calculation

**What they give you:** Beginning basis, distributions, income, losses, debt changes.
**What they ask:** Calculate ending basis.

**S-Corp Basis Order (each year):**

| Step | Action | Mnemonic Letter |
|------|--------|----------------|
| 1 | Start with beginning basis | **I** — Initial |
| 2 | + Additional contributions | **N** — New money in |
| 3 | + Share of income (including tax-exempt) | **C** — Cash from income |
| 4 | − Distributions | **O** — Out goes distributions |
| 5 | − Non-deductible, non-capital expenses | **M** — Minus non-deductible |
| 6 | − Losses & deductions (limited to remaining basis) | **E** — Expenses/losses last |

🧠 **Mnemonic: "INCOME" — Initial, New contributions, Cash-income, Out-distributions, Minus non-deductible, Expenses-losses**

**Partnership Basis** — Same concept but includes SHARE OF LIABILITIES (recourse and nonrecourse). This is the big difference from S-Corps.

#### TBS #3: Property Gain/Loss Classification

**What they give you:** List of assets sold with cost, depreciation, holding period, and selling price.
**What they ask:** Classify each as ordinary, §1231, §1245 recapture, or capital gain/loss.

**Decision Tree:**
```
Was it depreciable business property?
├── YES → §1231 property
│   ├── Was depreciation claimed? → §1245 recapture (ordinary up to depreciation taken)
│   └── Remaining gain → §1231 gain (capital rate)
├── NO → Was it held for investment?
│   ├── YES → Capital gain/loss (long-term if held >1 year)
│   └── NO → Ordinary gain/loss
```

🧠 **Mnemonic: "Depreciation Recapture Is Ordinary" — DRIO**

#### TBS #4: Entity Classification & Comparison

**What they give you:** Scenarios describing different business situations.
**What they ask:** Determine the best entity type, identify tax consequences.

**Quick Classification Guide:**
- Want limited liability + simplest tax? → **LLC taxed as partnership**
- Want to raise capital from many investors? → **C-Corporation**
- Want pass-through but limited liability, <100 owners? → **S-Corporation**
- Solo professional? → **Single-member LLC (disregarded entity) or S-Corp for SE tax savings**

#### TBS #5: Circular 230 / Ethics Scenarios

**What they give you:** A scenario where a tax practitioner faces an ethical decision.
**What they ask:** Identify violations, required actions, or appropriate standards.

**Key Rules to Apply:**
- Must exercise due diligence
- Must not take unreasonable positions (apply RSM standard)
- Must return client records promptly
- Cannot charge contingent fees on original returns
- Must advise client of penalties/errors discovered
- Must promptly notify client of IRS audit

## 6. 🧪 Diagnostic Quiz with Gap Analysis

✅ **Take this 20-question quiz without looking at notes.** Be honest. Your mistakes will show you exactly where to focus.

### The Quiz

**Individual Taxation (Questions 1–5)**

**Q1.** A taxpayer earned $80,000 in wages, $3,000 in bank interest, and received a $5,000 gift from a friend. What is their Gross Income?
- A) $88,000
- B) $83,000 ✅
- C) $80,000
- D) $85,000

*Answer: B) $83,000. Gifts are excluded from gross income (IRC §102). Wages + interest = $83,000.*

**Q2.** Which of the following is an "above-the-line" deduction (adjustment to income)?
- A) Mortgage interest
- B) State income taxes
- C) Student loan interest ✅
- D) Charitable contributions

*Answer: C) Student loan interest is an adjustment (above-the-line). The others are itemized deductions (below-the-line).*

**Q3.** The standard deduction for a Single filer in 2024 is:
- A) $12,950
- B) $13,850
- C) $14,600 ✅
- D) $29,200

*Answer: C) $14,600 for Single. $29,200 is MFJ.*

**Q4.** The Child Tax Credit is worth up to:
- A) $1,000
- B) $1,500
- C) $2,000 ✅
- D) $3,000

*Answer: C) $2,000 per qualifying child under 17.*

**Q5.** Self-employment tax consists of:
- A) 15.3% on all self-employment income
- B) 12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare on 92.35% of net SE income ✅
- C) 7.65% of net SE income
- D) 6.2% + 1.45% of gross SE income

*Answer: B) The SE tax is 15.3% (12.4% SS + 2.9% Medicare) on 92.35% of net SE income. The 92.35% adjustment represents the employer-equivalent portion.*

**Entity Taxation (Questions 6–10)**

**Q6.** Which entity faces double taxation?
- A) S-Corporation
- B) Partnership
- C) C-Corporation ✅
- D) Sole Proprietorship

*Answer: C) C-Corporation pays corporate tax, then shareholders pay tax on dividends.*

**Q7.** An S-Corporation can have a maximum of how many shareholders?
- A) 50
- B) 75
- C) 100 ✅
- D) Unlimited

*Answer: C) 100 shareholders maximum.*

**Q8.** In a partnership, a partner's basis is increased by:
- A) Partnership liabilities allocated to them ✅
- B) Distributions received
- C) Personal expenses of the partner
- D) Depreciation claimed by the partnership

*Answer: A) Share of partnership liabilities increases basis. This is unique to partnerships (not S-Corps).*

**Q9.** The correct order for loss limitation on pass-through entities is:
- A) Passive → Basis → At-Risk
- B) At-Risk → Basis → Passive
- C) Basis → At-Risk → Passive ✅
- D) Passive → At-Risk → Basis

*Answer: C) BARP — Basis → At-Risk → Passive → (Excess Business Loss).*

**Q10.** A C-Corporation's flat tax rate is:
- A) 15%
- B) 21% ✅
- C) 28%
- D) 35%

*Answer: B) 21% flat rate since TCJA (2018).*

**Property Transactions (Questions 11–14)**

**Q11.** You receive a gift of stock with a donor's basis of $10,000 and FMV of $8,000 at the date of gift. You sell it for $9,000. What is your gain or loss?
- A) $1,000 gain
- B) $1,000 loss
- C) $0 — no gain or loss ✅
- D) $9,000 gain

*Answer: C) $0. When FMV at gift date is LESS than donor's basis, there's a "dual basis" rule. For gain, use donor's basis ($10K — no gain since $9K < $10K). For loss, use FMV ($8K — no loss since $9K > $8K). You're in the middle → no gain or loss.*

**Q12.** Section 1245 depreciation recapture is taxed as:
- A) Capital gain
- B) Ordinary income ✅
- C) Section 1231 gain
- D) Tax-exempt income

*Answer: B) Ordinary income — the IRS takes back the depreciation benefit at ordinary rates.*

**Q13.** In a like-kind exchange (§1031), which property qualifies?
- A) Equipment for equipment
- B) Real property for real property held for business/investment ✅
- C) Stock for stock
- D) Personal residence for rental property

*Answer: B) After TCJA, §1031 only applies to real property held for productive use in a trade/business or for investment.*

**Q14.** An asset has an adjusted basis of $20,000. You sell it for $15,000 cash plus the buyer assumes your $8,000 loan. What is the amount realized?
- A) $15,000
- B) $20,000
- C) $23,000 ✅
- D) $28,000

*Answer: C) $23,000. Amount realized = cash ($15K) + debt relief ($8K) = $23,000. Gain = $23K − $20K = $3,000.*

**Business Law (Questions 15–17)**

**Q15.** A valid contract requires all of the following EXCEPT:
- A) Offer
- B) Acceptance
- C) Consideration
- D) A written document ✅

*Answer: D) Not all contracts need to be written (only those under the Statute of Frauds — MYLEGS). Oral contracts can be valid.*

**Q16.** Under the Statute of Frauds, which contracts MUST be in writing?
- A) A contract for the sale of goods over $500 ✅
- B) A contract for services that can be completed in one month
- C) A verbal agreement between friends
- D) An employment contract for 6 months

*Answer: A) UCC: contracts for sale of goods ≥$500 must be in writing. Mnemonic: MYLEGS — Marriage, Year (>1 yr), Land, Executor, Goods (≥$500), Surety.*

🧠 **Mnemonic: "MY LEGS" — Marriage, Year (can't complete within 1 year), Land, Executor, Goods ≥$500, Surety**

**Q17.** In bankruptcy, which creditor is paid FIRST?
- A) General unsecured creditors
- B) Secured creditors ✅
- C) Government tax claims
- D) Employees owed wages

*Answer: B) Secured creditors are paid first from their collateral. Then: Admin → Domestic Support → Wages (limited) → Tax → General Unsecured.*

**Ethics & Circular 230 (Questions 18–20)**

**Q18.** Under Circular 230, the minimum standard for a disclosed, non-tax-shelter position is:
- A) More likely than not
- B) Substantial authority
- C) Reasonable basis ✅
- D) Frivolous

*Answer: C) Reasonable basis (~1/3 chance of being sustained) for disclosed non-tax-shelter positions.*

**Q19.** Can a tax practitioner charge a contingent fee for preparing an original tax return?
- A) Yes, always
- B) No, never for original returns ✅
- C) Only if the client agrees in writing
- D) Only if the fee is under $5,000

*Answer: B) Circular 230 prohibits contingent fees for original returns. Contingent fees are allowed for amended returns, refund claims, and IRS examination matters.*

**Q20.** The IRS statute of limitations for assessment is generally:
- A) 2 years
- B) 3 years ✅
- C) 5 years
- D) 7 years

*Answer: B) Generally 3 years from the LATER of the filing date or due date. Exceptions: 6 years if >25% gross income omission; unlimited if fraud or no return filed.*

## 8. ❓ 5 Concept-Building Follow-Up Questions

These questions are designed to push your understanding deeper. Try answering them without notes first, then check.

### Question 1: The "Dual Basis" Trap

**A friend gives you stock she bought for $15,000. At the time of the gift, the FMV is $10,000. She paid no gift tax. You sell it for $12,000. What is your gain or loss?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: $0 — No gain or loss.**

This is the "dual basis" or "split basis" rule for gifts where FMV < donor's basis at gift date:
- For calculating a GAIN, use the donor's basis ($15,000). Since $12,000 < $15,000 → no gain.
- For calculating a LOSS, use the FMV at gift date ($10,000). Since $12,000 > $10,000 → no loss.
- The selling price ($12,000) falls in the "no-man's land" between $10,000 and $15,000.

✅ **Think of it like a hallway between two doors.** The "gain door" is at $15,000 and the "loss door" is at $10,000. If you sell for any price between the two doors, you're stuck in the hallway — no gain, no loss.
</details>

### Question 2: S-Corp vs Partnership Basis

**Why can partners include their share of partnership debt in their basis, but S-Corp shareholders cannot include corporate debt in theirs? What is the conceptual reason?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: It comes down to personal liability.**

In a partnership, partners (especially general partners) are personally liable for partnership debts. If the partnership can't pay, creditors can come after the partners personally. Since they bear the economic risk, they get basis for that risk.

In an S-Corp, shareholders have limited liability — corporate debts are the corporation's problem, not the shareholders'. Since shareholders don't bear the economic risk of corporate debt, they don't get basis for it. The only way to increase S-Corp basis through debt is direct loans FROM the shareholder TO the corporation.

✅ **Think of it like co-signing a loan.** If you co-sign your friend's loan (partnership debt), you're on the hook — so the IRS lets you count it. If your friend's company borrows money and you're just a stockholder, you're NOT on the hook — so no basis for you.
</details>

### Question 3: Ethics Escalation

**A CPA discovers that a client's prior-year return has a material error that the client refuses to correct. The error is not the CPA's fault. What must the CPA do under Circular 230? Can the CPA notify the IRS directly?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer:**
1. The CPA must **advise the client** of the error and its consequences (including potential penalties).
2. If the client refuses to correct it, the CPA should **consider withdrawing** from the engagement.
3. The CPA **CANNOT notify the IRS directly** — client confidentiality is paramount. There is no duty to "rat out" the client.
4. However, the CPA **cannot sign** the current-year return if it is affected by the prior-year error.
5. The CPA should document everything in writing.

✅ **Think of it like being a doctor.** You can tell a patient they need surgery, but you can't force them. And you definitely can't call their family without permission. But you CAN refuse to keep treating them.
</details>

### Question 4: Like-Kind Exchange Timing

**In a deferred like-kind exchange under §1031, what are the two critical deadlines, and what happens if you miss either one?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer:**
1. **45-Day Identification Period:** You must identify potential replacement properties within 45 days of transferring your relinquished property. You can identify up to 3 properties (the "3-property rule") regardless of value, or any number if their total FMV doesn't exceed 200% of the relinquished property's FMV.
2. **180-Day Exchange Period:** You must receive the replacement property within 180 days of the transfer (or the due date of your tax return, whichever is earlier).

**If you miss EITHER deadline:** The entire exchange is disqualified. The transaction is treated as a taxable sale, and you recognize all gain immediately. No do-overs.

✅ **Think of it like a basketball shot clock.** You have 45 seconds (days) to call your shot (identify the property) and 180 seconds (days) to make the basket (close the deal). Miss either clock → turnover (taxable event).
</details>

### Question 5: Bankruptcy Priority Challenge

**A company files for Chapter 7 bankruptcy. Arrange these claims in the correct order of priority: (a) IRS tax claim for $50,000, (b) Employee wages of $15,000, (c) Secured bank loan of $200,000, (d) General trade creditors owed $80,000, (e) Trustee administrative fees of $20,000, (f) Domestic support obligation of $30,000.**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer — in order of priority:**

1. **Secured bank loan ($200,000)** — Paid first from collateral; any deficiency becomes unsecured
2. **Trustee administrative fees ($20,000)** — Running the bankruptcy costs money
3. **Domestic support obligation ($30,000)** — Child support/alimony is prioritized
4. **Employee wages ($15,000)** — Within limits (earned within 180 days pre-filing, up to $15,150 per employee)
5. **IRS tax claim ($50,000)** — Government taxes come after wages and domestic support
6. **General trade creditors ($80,000)** — Last in line, often get pennies on the dollar

🧠 **Remember SADTU** (expanded): **S**ecured → **A**dmin → **D**omestic support → **T**axes (with wages fitting between domestic support and taxes) → **U**nsecured

✅ **Think of it like a buffet line.** Secured creditors eat first (they brought their own plate — the collateral). The event organizers (admin) eat next. Then family obligations. Then the government. General creditors get whatever's left — usually crumbs.
</details>

## 10. 🔄 Challenge Your Assumptions — Reframe the Problem

✅ **Most candidates approach REG with the wrong mental model.** Here's how to reframe:

### Assumption #1: "I need to memorize everything in the Internal Revenue Code"

**Reframe:** You need to memorize about 200 rules and know where to find the rest. The exam provides the Authoritative Literature — use it. Focus on the 80/20: the top 200 rules cover 80% of questions. Everything else can be looked up.

### Assumption #2: "MCQs and TBS require different skills"

**Reframe:** They test the SAME knowledge — MCQs test recognition (do you recognize the right answer when you see it?), while TBS test application (can you produce the right answer from scratch?). If you deeply understand a concept, both formats become easy. Don't study MCQs and TBS separately — study the underlying concepts, then practice both formats.

### Assumption #3: "Business Law is the easiest section because it's not about numbers"

**Reframe:** Business Law is deceptively challenging because it requires precise legal vocabulary and distinction between similar concepts (e.g., express vs. implied authority, void vs. voidable contracts). Many candidates score poorly here because they don't take it seriously. Give it the full 5–6 days it deserves.

### Assumption #4: "I should study topics in the order of their exam weight"

**Reframe:** Study topics in order of DEPENDENCY, not weight. Individual taxation teaches the foundational tax formula that entity taxation builds on. Property transactions teach basis, which is required for entity taxation. Study in this order: Individuals → Property → Entities → Business Law → Ethics. Each layer builds on the previous one.

### Assumption #5: "A 63% pass rate means this exam is easy"

**Reframe:** The 63% pass rate includes retakers who failed previously and studied more the second time. The FIRST-TIME pass rate is closer to 50%. And you need a 75 to pass on a 0–99 scale. Don't let the aggregate number give you a false sense of security. Study as if the pass rate is 40%.

### Assumption #6: "I should spend equal time on all five content areas"

**Reframe:** Weight your study time by exam weight AND personal weakness. If Entity Taxation is 23–33% of the exam but it's your weakest area, spend 40% of your time there. The ROI on improving your weakest area is always higher than polishing your strongest.
