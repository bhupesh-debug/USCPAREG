#### Topic 1: Individual Tax Formula
**Gross Income → AGI → Taxable Income → Tax → Credits → Amount Owed/Refund**

This is your GPS. Every single individual tax question on the exam is asking: *Where in this formula does this item belong?* Know it cold. Know it backwards. Know it in your sleep.

✅ **The Formula Broken Down:**

```
Gross Income (all income from any source)
   MINUS: Adjustments to Income (above-the-line deductions)
= Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)
   MINUS: Greater of Standard Deduction OR Itemized Deductions
   MINUS: Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction
= Taxable Income
   TIMES: Tax Rate (from brackets)
= Gross Tax Liability
   MINUS: Credits (nonrefundable, then refundable)
= Net Tax Liability
   MINUS: Payments Made (withholding, estimated tax)
= Amount Owed OR Refund
```

🧠 **Memory Anchor:** Think of this as water flowing downhill. Every step removes more water (reduces income). Credits are the last dam before the river reaches the IRS.

**Why it matters on the exam:** The AICPA tests every element of this formula — from obscure gross income inclusions to the sequencing of credits vs. payments. If you know this formula perfectly, you instantly know *where* to look for the answer to 70% of individual tax questions.

#### Topic 2: Filing Status & Dependents

Filing status determines your standard deduction, tax brackets, and credit eligibility. The five statuses in order of most favorable to least:
1. Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)
2. Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS) — same rates as MFJ
3. Head of Household (HOH)
4. Single
5. Married Filing Separately (MFS) — least favorable

🧠 **CARES — Qualifying Child Tests:**
- **C** — Child (relationship: child, stepchild, eligible foster, sibling)
- **A** — Age (under 19, or under 24 if full-time student, or permanently disabled)
- **R** — Residency (lived with taxpayer more than half the year)
- **E** — Elimination (cannot be used as qualifying child of more than one person — tiebreaker rules apply)
- **S** — Support (child cannot provide more than half of their own support)

🧠 **SUPORT — Qualifying Relative Tests:**
- **S** — Support (taxpayer must provide more than 50% of the person's support)
- **U** — Under gross income limit ($4,700 for 2024)
- **P** — Part of household OR related (broad relationship list)
- **O** — Only one taxpayer can claim
- **R** — Relationship (or member of household)
- **T** — Tax return (cannot themselves file a joint return)

✅ **Key Exam Trap:** Head of Household requires the taxpayer to be UNMARRIED (or considered unmarried), pay more than 50% of household costs, and have a qualifying person live there MORE than half the year. Many questions test whether the child must be the taxpayer's own — it does NOT have to be. A qualifying relative (like a parent in a separate home) can qualify for HOH if the taxpayer pays more than 50% of the parent's household costs.

#### Topic 3: S-Corporation Shareholder Basis

S-Corp basis is one of the most tested topics on the entire REG exam. It appears in MCQs and is almost guaranteed in TBS simulations.

**Starting basis = Amount paid for stock (or FMV at conversion)**

🧠 **ACID-NL — The Adjustment Order (Process This Sequence Every Year):**
1. **A** — Add Separately stated income items (capital gains, tax-exempt income)
2. **C** — Add Corporate ordinary income (nonseparately stated)
3. **I** — Add Additional investments made during the year
4. **D** — Decrease by Distributions (cash or property — at FMV)
5. **N** — Decrease by Nonseparately stated losses (ordinary loss)
6. **L** — Decrease by Losses separately stated (capital losses, charitable contributions)

**Basis cannot go below zero.** If losses exceed basis, they are suspended and carried forward until basis is restored.

✅ **The Three Basis Limits (In Order):**
1. **Stock Basis** — reduces losses first
2. **Debt Basis** — only loans the shareholder makes DIRECTLY to the corporation (not guaranteed third-party loans)
3. **At-Risk** — further limits losses to amounts truly at economic risk
4. **Passive Activity Rules** — final filter for passive investors

**AAA (Accumulated Adjustments Account):** Tracks the S-corp's cumulative income/loss since S election. Distributions come from AAA first (tax-free to extent of basis), then AEP (from prior C-corp years, taxable as dividend), then excess basis (capital gain), then return of capital.

#### Topic 4: Partnership Outside Basis

Partnership basis (called "outside basis") is equally critical and follows similar but distinct rules.

**Initial outside basis = Cash contributed + Adjusted basis of property contributed + Share of partnership liabilities assumed**

🧠 **CLIP — Annual Increases to Outside Basis:**
- **C** — Cash contributions
- **L** — Liabilities assumed by the partnership (partner's share)
- **I** — Income items (including tax-exempt income)
- **P** — Partnership income allocated to partner

🧠 **DLL — Annual Decreases to Outside Basis:**
- **D** — Distributions received
- **L** — Liabilities relieved (partner's share reduced)
- **L** — Losses allocated to partner

**Critical Difference from S-Corps:** In partnerships, partners share in **all** partnership liabilities (general partners share recourse and nonrecourse; limited partners share only nonrecourse). This means partners can have basis from liabilities they never personally borrowed — which allows loss deductions that S-corp shareholders cannot take.

✅ **Guaranteed Payments:** Payments to partners for services or capital, regardless of partnership income. Deductible by the partnership, ordinary income to the partner. They do NOT increase the partner's outside basis — they are treated like compensation, not a contribution.

#### Topic 5: C-Corporation Distributions & Earnings and Profits (E&P)

🧠 **DRC Waterfall — How Distributions Are Characterized:**
1. **D** — Dividend: Taxable as ordinary income to the extent of current + accumulated E&P
2. **R** — Return of Capital: Reduces shareholder's stock basis (tax-free) once E&P is exhausted
3. **C** — Capital Gain: Any excess above stock basis is capital gain

**Current E&P is allocated pro-rata** to distributions made throughout the year. **Accumulated E&P** is applied chronologically to distributions after current E&P is exhausted.

✅ **Key Distinctions:**
- Dividends received by corporate shareholders may be eligible for the **Dividends Received Deduction (DRD)**: 50% if <20% ownership, 65% if ≥20%, 100% if ≥80% affiliated group
- **Constructive Dividends:** Excessive compensation, personal expenses paid by corporation, below-market loans to shareholders — all treated as dividends
- **Section 351:** No gain/loss recognized when property is transferred to a corporation if the transferors control ≥80% immediately after the transfer

#### Topic 6: Property Basis Rules

Basis is the single most important number in all of tax law. Every gain, loss, depreciation, and exchange calculation starts here.

| Acquisition Method | Basis Rule | Key Trap |
|---|---|---|
| Purchase | Cost (FMV paid) | Include assumption of liabilities |
| Gift — FMV > Donor's Basis | Donor's carryover basis | Holding period also carries over |
| Gift — FMV < Donor's Basis | **Double Basis Rule** | Gain basis = donor's basis; Loss basis = FMV on gift date |
| Inherited Property | FMV on date of death (Step-Up) | Holding period = automatically long-term |
| Like-Kind Exchange | Carryover basis +/- boot | Boot received triggers gain |
| Property Converted to Business | Lower of adjusted basis OR FMV on conversion date | For loss purposes only |

🧠 **The Double Basis Rule for Gifts (Most Tested Gift Trap):**
- If you sell for MORE than donor's basis → use donor's basis (calculate gain)
- If you sell for LESS than FMV on gift date → use FMV on gift date (calculate loss)
- If you sell between FMV and donor's basis → **NO gain, NO loss** (the "dead zone")

#### Topic 7: MACRS Depreciation & Section 179

**MACRS (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System)** — the depreciation system used for tax purposes.

| Asset Class | Recovery Period | Method |
|---|---|---|
| Automobiles, computers | 5-year | 200% DB switching to SL |
| Office furniture, fixtures | 7-year | 200% DB switching to SL |
| Land improvements, fences | 15-year | 150% DB switching to SL |
| Residential rental property | 27.5-year | Straight-line |
| Nonresidential real property | 39-year | Straight-line |

**Half-Year Convention:** Most property uses this — treated as placed in service mid-year. **Mid-Quarter Convention** applies if >40% of depreciable property is placed in service in the final quarter.

**Section 179 — Immediate Expensing:**
- 2024 limit: $1,220,000 (phase-out begins at $3,050,000 of placed-in-service property)
- Cannot create or increase a net operating loss (limited to business taxable income)
- NOT available for real property (land and buildings), but IS available for qualified improvement property

**Bonus Depreciation (Section 168(k)):**
- 60% for 2024 (phasing down — was 100% through 2022, 80% in 2023)
- Applies AFTER Section 179, BEFORE regular MACRS
- CAN create a net operating loss (unlike Section 179)
- Applies to new AND used qualified property

#### Topic 8: Gain/Loss Character — Section 1231, 1245, 1250

This is the hierarchy that determines whether your gain is capital or ordinary. Apply it IN ORDER.

**Step 1: Section 1245 Recapture**
- Personal property (equipment, machinery) — recapture all depreciation taken as **ordinary income** first
- Applies to gain to the extent of depreciation deducted

**Step 2: Section 1250 Recapture**
- Real property — recapture "additional" depreciation (actual MACRS depreciation minus what straight-line would have been) as ordinary income
- Under current MACRS (all straight-line for real property), §1250 recapture is usually zero for regular tax — BUT "unrecaptured §1250 gain" (28.8% rate) still applies

**Step 3: Section 1231 Net Gain or Loss**
- All remaining gain on business assets (land, buildings, equipment held >1 year) after recapture
- Net §1231 gains → **long-term capital gain** (preferential rates)
- Net §1231 losses → **ordinary loss** (fully deductible — best of both worlds)
- **§1231 Lookback Rule:** If you had net §1231 losses in the prior 5 years, current §1231 gains are recharacterized as ordinary income to the extent of those prior losses

🧠 **The Recapture Order: "1245 Before 1250, Then 1231 Takes the Floor"**

#### Topic 9: Contracts — Formation & Statute of Frauds

**Contract Formation — The 4 Requirements:**
1. **Offer** — definite terms, communicated to offeree
2. **Acceptance** — mirror image rule (common law), or any reasonable manner (UCC)
3. **Consideration** — bargained-for exchange, legal detriment, past consideration is NOT valid
4. **Capacity** — minors (under 18) and mentally incompetent parties may void contracts

**Common Law vs. UCC Article 2:**
- Common law: governs services and real estate contracts
- UCC Article 2: governs sale of goods (movable personal property)
- Hybrid contracts: apply the "predominant purpose" test

🧠 **MY LEGS — Statute of Frauds (Contracts That Must Be in Writing):**
- **M** — Marriage — contracts in consideration of marriage
- **Y** — Year — contracts that cannot be performed within one year
- **L** — Land — contracts for the sale or transfer of real property
- **E** — Executor — promises by an executor to pay estate debts personally
- **G** — Goods over $500 — UCC Article 2 (sale of goods ≥$500)
- **S** — Surety/Guaranty — promise to pay another's debt

✅ **Key Exam Traps on Statute of Frauds:**
- A one-year employment contract signed TODAY for work starting in 6 months → must be in writing (cannot be completed within one year from signing)
- A lifetime contract could theoretically be completed within one year (if the person dies) → need NOT be in writing
- Part performance can take a land contract out of the Statute of Frauds in some jurisdictions

**Contract Defenses (Make Contract Voidable or Void):**
- Mutual mistake of material fact → voidable by either party
- Unilateral mistake → generally NOT a defense (unless other party knew)
- Fraud, duress, undue influence → voidable by injured party
- Illegality → void ab initio (no contract at all)

#### Topic 10: Tax Credits

Credits are dollar-for-dollar reductions in tax liability — far more valuable than deductions.

**American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) vs. Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC):**

| Feature | AOTC | LLC |
|---|---|---|
| Maximum credit | $2,500 (100% first $2K, 25% next $2K) | $2,000 (20% of up to $10K) |
| Years of school | First 4 years only | Unlimited |
| Half-time enrollment? | Required | Not required |
| Refundable? | 40% refundable (up to $1,000) | Not refundable |
| Felony drug conviction? | Disqualifies | Does NOT disqualify |
| Phase-out (MFJ, 2024) | $160K–$180K MAGI | $160K–$180K MAGI |

**Child Tax Credit:** $2,000 per qualifying child under 17. Phase-out: $400K (MFJ), $200K (all others). Up to $1,600 refundable (Additional Child Tax Credit) for 2024.

**Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC):** Refundable credit for lower-income working individuals/families. Investment income limit: $11,000 for 2024. Cannot file MFS.

**Foreign Tax Credit:** Taxes paid to foreign governments are creditable (or deductible if elected). Limitation = US tax × (foreign income / worldwide income).

**Child and Dependent Care Credit:** 20–35% of up to $3,000 (one dependent) or $6,000 (two+). NOT refundable for most taxpayers.

#### Topic 11: Circular 230 & Tax Preparer Penalties

**Circular 230** governs practice before the IRS. It applies to attorneys, CPAs, enrolled agents, and enrolled actuaries.

**Competence Standards Under Circular 230:**
- Must have appropriate knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation
- Must not charge unconscionable fees
- Must not delay proceedings
- Must promptly return client records
- Conflicts of interest require informed written consent from all affected clients

🧠 **RSM Standards Ladder — Preparer Penalty Thresholds:**
- **R** — Reasonable Basis: Lowest standard. 20% penalty if position not disclosed; avoids 20% penalty if disclosed on return
- **S** — Substantial Authority: Higher standard (~40% chance of prevailing). Avoids 20% accuracy-related penalty without disclosure
- **M** — More Likely Than Not (>50% chance): Required for tax shelters and reportable transactions to avoid 20% penalty

**Tax Preparer Penalties (Section 6694):**
- Understatement due to unreasonable position: Greater of $1,000 or 50% of income derived from the return
- Willful or reckless conduct: Greater of $5,000 or 75% of income derived from the return

**Taxpayer Penalties:**
- Failure to file: 5% per month, max 25% of unpaid tax
- Failure to pay: 0.5% per month, max 25% of unpaid tax
- Accuracy-related penalty: 20% of underpayment
- Fraud penalty: 75% of underpayment
- Substantial understatement: >$5,000 or >10% of correct tax

---

#### Topic 12: Statute of Limitations

🧠 **3-6-FOREVER:**
- **3 years** — Standard assessment period (from later of return due date or actual filing date)
- **6 years** — Substantial omission: omitted >25% of gross income from return
- **FOREVER** — No statute of limitations when: (1) fraudulent return filed, (2) no return filed at all

**Additional Key Periods:**
- Refund claims: 3 years from filing OR 2 years from payment, whichever is LATER
- Bad debt/worthless securities: 7 years from due date of return
- Employment tax: 3 years standard; no limit for fraud

✅ **Exam Trap:** The 6-year rule is for omission of gross income, NOT understatement of tax. If a taxpayer misstates deductions but properly reports all income, the standard 3-year period applies.

## SECTION 2: THE 30-DAY LEARNING ROADMAP
### Your Day-by-Day Battle Plan (3–4 hours/day weekdays, 6–8 hours/day weekends)

The following roadmap is designed for 140 hours of focused study. Follow it in sequence — each week builds on the last. The structure is: Learn → Practice → Review.

---

### WEEK 1 (Days 1–7): Foundation — Individual Taxation

**Day 1 — Filing Status & Dependents**
- Master the 5 filing statuses and their standard deductions
- Memorize CARES (qualifying child) and SUPORT (qualifying relative) mnemonics
- Practice: 30 MCQs on filing status and dependents
- Target: 70%+ accuracy before moving on

**Day 2 — Gross Income**
- The 14 gross income categories — all income from any source unless specifically excluded
- Key exclusions: gifts and inheritances, life insurance proceeds, workers' comp, employer health insurance, municipal bond interest, scholarships (tuition only)
- Key inclusions most students miss: alimony (pre-2019 agreements), cancellation of debt, punitive damages, gambling winnings
- Practice: 30 MCQs on gross income inclusions and exclusions

**Day 3 — Adjustments to Income (Above-the-Line Deductions)**

🧠 **MISHAP SEA — Above-the-Line Deductions:**
- **M** — Moving expenses (active military only, post-2017)
- **I** — IRA deductions (traditional IRA, limits apply)
- **S** — Student loan interest (up to $2,500, phase-out applies)
- **H** — Health Savings Account (HSA) contributions
- **A** — Alimony paid (pre-2019 agreements only)
- **P** — Penalty on early withdrawal of savings
- **S** — Self-employed health insurance premiums
- **E** — Educator expenses (up to $300)
- **A** — Alimony + SE tax deduction (50% of SE tax deducted above-the-line)

- Practice: 30 MCQs on AGI adjustments

**Day 4 — Itemized Deductions & Standard Deduction**

🧠 **MAGIC CT — Itemized Deductions:**
- **M** — Medical expenses (>7.5% AGI floor — only the excess is deductible)
- **A** — Alimony paid (pre-2019 divorce agreements)
- **G** — Gambling losses (deductible only to extent of gambling winnings)
- **I** — Interest expense (mortgage interest limited to $750K acquisition debt; investment interest limited to net investment income)
- **C** — Charitable contributions (cash: 60% AGI limit; capital gain property: 30% AGI limit; carryforward 5 years)
- **C** — Casualty and theft losses (only federally declared disasters, post-2017)
- **T** — Taxes — SALT (state and local taxes: capped at $10,000 for all combined)

- Standard deduction 2024: $14,600 (single), $29,200 (MFJ), $21,900 (HOH)
- Additional standard deduction: $1,950 (single, over 65/blind), $1,550 (MFJ, over 65/blind per spouse)
- Practice: 30 MCQs on itemized vs. standard deduction strategy

**Day 5 — Tax Credits**
- AOTC vs. LLC (memorize the comparison table from Section 1)
- Child Tax Credit mechanics and phase-outs
- EITC: earned income requirement, investment income limit, no MFS
- Child and Dependent Care: 20–35% × up to $3K/$6K
- Premium Tax Credit: marketplace health insurance, income 100–400% FPL
- Practice: 30 MCQs on credits

**Day 6 (Weekend) — QBI, Other Taxes, Estimated Payments**
- QBI Deduction (Section 199A): 20% of qualified business income. Phase-in of W-2/property limits at $383,900 (MFJ) and $191,950 (single) for 2024. Specified service trades excluded above threshold.
- Self-Employment Tax: 15.3% on net SE income (92.35% of net profit). 50% deductible above-the-line.
- Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT): 3.8% on lesser of NII or MAGI excess over $250K (MFJ)/$200K (single)
- Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT): Add back preferences and adjustments. Exemption $137,000 (MFJ), $88,100 (single) for 2024.
- Estimated Tax Payments: Safe harbor = 100% of prior year tax (110% if prior year AGI >$150K) OR 90% of current year tax. Underpayment penalty applies quarterly.
- Practice: 50 MCQs (mix of all Week 1 topics). Complete first TBS practice: Form 1040 simulation.

**Day 7 (Weekend) — Full Week 1 Review**
- Comprehensive review of all individual tax topics
- Practice: 75 MCQs cumulative (focus on areas where accuracy fell below 70%)
- Review every wrong answer — write down the rule in your error log
- Score target: 75%+ overall before entering Week 2

### WEEK 2 (Days 8–14): Property Transactions + Beginning Entity Taxation

**Day 8 — Basis of Assets**
- Cost basis, gift basis (double-basis rule), inherited basis (step-up to FMV at death)
- Converted property: lower of adjusted basis or FMV at conversion
- Allocating cost basis in lump-sum purchases (allocate by relative FMV)
- Property received for services: FMV is basis
- Practice: 30 MCQs on basis determination

**Day 9 — MACRS Depreciation, Section 179, Bonus Depreciation**
- Memorize the MACRS asset class table (5-year, 7-year, 15-year, 27.5-year, 39-year)
- Half-year vs. mid-quarter convention — when does each apply?
- Section 179: $1,220,000 limit, taxable income limitation, phase-out at $3,050,000
- Bonus depreciation: 60% for 2024, no taxable income limit
- Listed property: luxury autos, computers — specific limitations apply
- Practice: 30 MCQs on depreciation

**Day 10 — Gain/Loss Character: Sections 1231, 1245, 1250**
- Apply the three-step recapture hierarchy from Section 1 (Topic 8)
- Work through computation problems: calculate accumulated depreciation, §1245 recapture, remaining §1231 gain
- The §1231 lookback rule: prior 5-year net §1231 losses recharacterize current gains
- Capital gain rates: 0%, 15%, 20% (based on income); §1231 = LTCG rates; §1245 = ordinary rates
- Practice: 30 MCQs on character of gain/loss

**Day 11 — Like-Kind Exchanges, Wash Sales, Related Parties**
- §1031 Like-Kind Exchange: No gain/loss recognized on exchange of like-kind real property (post-2017: real property only; personal property no longer qualifies)
- Boot received = gain recognized (lesser of boot received or realized gain)
- Basis in replacement property = basis in surrendered property − boot received + gain recognized
- Wash sale: Cannot recognize loss if substantially identical securities purchased within 30 days before or after sale. Disallowed loss adds to basis of new securities.
- Related party rules: §267 disallows losses on sales between related parties (family members, >50% ownership entity). Gains on related party sales = ordinary income.
- Practice: 30 MCQs on exchanges and special transactions

**Day 12 — C-Corporations: Formation, DRD, E&P**
- §351 tax-free formation: transferors receive ≥80% control immediately after; no gain/loss on transfer
- Boot in §351: gain recognized (not loss)
- Dividends Received Deduction: 50%/65%/100% — memorize the ownership thresholds
- Computing E&P: Start with taxable income, add back tax-exempt income, subtract federal income taxes paid, adjust for timing differences (MACRS vs. straight-line, §179 expensing, installment sales)
- Practice: 30 MCQs on C-corp basics

**Day 13 (Weekend) — C-Corp Distributions, Constructive Dividends, Accumulated Earnings Tax**
- DRC waterfall mechanics (from Section 1, Topic 5)
- Liquidating distributions: shareholder recognizes gain/loss; basis becomes FMV received
- Constructive dividends: excessive compensation, personal use of corporate property, below-market loans (imputed interest rules under §7872)
- Accumulated Earnings Tax: 20% on accumulated earnings >$250,000 ($150,000 for personal service corporations) beyond reasonable business needs
- Personal Holding Company Tax: 20% on undistributed PHC income (≥60% passive income, ≥5 shareholders own >50%)
- Practice: 50 MCQs (C-corp focus). Complete TBS: property basis and gain/loss character simulation.

**Day 14 (Weekend) — Full Review Weeks 1–2**
- Practice: 100 MCQs cumulative (mix of individual tax and property/C-corp)
- Identify your three weakest topics from Weeks 1–2
- Write three-sentence summaries of each weak topic in your own words
- Score target: 75%+ before entering Week 3

---

### WEEK 3 (Days 15–21): Entity Taxation + Business Law

**Day 15 — S-Corporations: Election, Qualifications, Termination**

🧠 **S-QUID — S-Corp Eligibility Requirements:**
- **S** — Small business corporation (≤100 shareholders)
- **Q** — Qualified shareholders (US citizens/residents, certain trusts — no partnerships, no corporations)
- **U** — Uniform class of stock (only ONE class of stock, though voting differences OK)
- **I** — Ineligible corporations excluded (certain financial institutions, insurance companies)
- **D** — Domestic corporation (incorporated in the US)

- S election: Form 2553, due by 15th day of 3rd month of tax year; unanimous consent of all shareholders required
- Termination: voluntary revocation (>50% of shares), involuntary (violating eligibility), passive investment income >25% of gross receipts for 3 consecutive years (if AEP exists)
- Practice: 30 MCQs on S-corp eligibility and elections

**Day 16 — S-Corp Basis, AAA, and Distributions**
- Apply ACID-NL (from Section 1, Topic 3) to multi-year basis computations
- AAA tracks earnings since S election (not cumulative, can go negative from losses/deductions)
- Distribution ordering from AAA: AAA → AEP (if any, taxable as dividend) → remaining basis (return of capital) → excess (capital gain)
- Debt basis: only available from direct shareholder loans to corporation; restored by income before stock basis
- Practice: 30 MCQs + basis computation problems

**Day 17 — Partnerships: Formation and Outside Basis**
- §721: No gain/loss recognized when property contributed to partnership (exceptions for disguised sales, investment companies)
- Inside basis = partnership's adjusted basis in assets; Outside basis = partner's basis in partnership interest
- CLIP increases, DLL decreases (from Section 1, Topic 4)
- Liability sharing: general partners share recourse liabilities in loss-sharing ratio; limited partners share only nonrecourse liabilities
- §754 election: step up inside basis when partner sells interest or dies (eliminates basis disparities)
- Practice: 30 MCQs on partnership formation and basis

**Day 18 — Partnership Operations: Guaranteed Payments, Hot Assets**
- Guaranteed payments: ordinary income to recipient, deductible by partnership, do NOT increase basis
- Self-employment tax applies to guaranteed payments and general partner's distributive share
- Hot assets (§751): when a partner sells their interest, allocate gain/loss from unrealized receivables and inventory to ordinary income (not capital gain). This prevents conversion of ordinary income to capital gain through partnership interest sales.
- Substantial economic effect: allocations must have economic reality to be respected by IRS
- Special allocations: can be made if they have substantial economic effect
- Practice: 30 MCQs on partnership operations

**Day 19 — Business Law: Contracts, Agency**
- Contracts: Master all content from Section 1, Topic 9 (formation, MY LEGS, defenses)
- Third-party contracts: assignment of rights (generally permitted without consent), delegation of duties (permitted unless personal service), third-party beneficiaries (intended vs. incidental)

🧠 **LOCAN — Agency Relationships (When is Principal Liable?):**
- **L** — Liability exists when agent has express, implied, or apparent authority
- **O** — Ostensible/apparent authority: principal's conduct led third party to reasonably believe agent had authority
- **C** — Capacity: both principal and agent must have contractual capacity
- **A** — Agent acting within scope of employment (respondeat superior for torts)
- **N** — Notification to principal: agent must disclose, account, and obey lawful instructions

- Practice: 30 MCQs on contracts and agency

**Day 20 (Weekend) — Agency Deep Dive, Debtor-Creditor, Business Structures**

## SECTION 3: THE PROFESSIONAL MINDSET
### The Mental Framework of 90+ Scorers

The difference between a 75 and a 90 is not intelligence — it is the way a top scorer approaches problems. These are the mental models that separate average from elite.

---

### "The Tax Formula Is Your GPS"

Every question — every single one — maps back to the tax formula. When you read a question, your first thought should be: *Where in the formula does this item land?* Is it above AGI? Below AGI? Does it affect taxable income or tax liability? Is it a credit that comes after the tax calculation?

Top scorers don't read questions and think "what rule applies here?" They read questions and immediately locate the question on the formula map. The formula is not something you memorize once — it is the cognitive framework through which you see all individual taxation.

---

### "Basis Is the Language"

If you master basis — how it is established, adjusted, and lost — you master approximately 60% of REG. Every property transaction, every entity calculation, every depreciation problem, every exchange begins with basis. It is the foundational number of all of tax law.

When you see any REG question, ask yourself: *What is the basis of the asset or interest involved? Has it been adjusted? What event triggered the adjustment?* Even if the question is not explicitly about basis, basis is usually hiding in the answer.

---

### "Think Like an IRS Agent"

For every transaction in a REG question, ask three questions in sequence:
1. **What's taxable?** (Is this gross income? Is there an exclusion?)
2. **What's the basis?** (What number do we use to compute gain or loss?)
3. **What's the character?** (Is this ordinary income, capital gain, §1231 gain, recapture?)

This three-question discipline works for individual taxation, entity taxation, and property transactions alike. An IRS agent reviewing a return would ask exactly these three questions for every line item.

---

### "The 3-Second Rule"

When you read a REG MCQ, you should be able to identify the concept being tested within 3 seconds of finishing the question stem. "This is an S-corp basis question." "This is testing §1231 recapture." "This is a Statute of Frauds question."

If it takes you longer than 3 seconds, you do not know the material well enough — you are guessing rather than recognizing. The 3-second rule is your diagnostic: when you miss it, you know exactly what to study.

---

### "MCQs Train Speed, TBS Train Depth"

These are two completely different skills. Do not practice them the same way.

**MCQ practice** builds pattern recognition and speed. When you do MCQs, focus on identifying the concept quickly, eliminating clearly wrong answers, and moving on. Review wrong answers immediately after each set.

**TBS practice** builds depth, integration, and precision. When you do TBS, slow down. Read the requirement first. Map out your computation before entering a single number. Check your work. TBS questions are scored at the task level — a systematic error (like using the wrong basis amount) can cost you multiple points across an entire simulation.

Aim for 70%+ on MCQs and 65%+ on TBS in practice. These are realistic passing-level benchmarks.

---

### Growth Mindset: REG Is Beatable

REG has the highest pass rate of all four core CPA sections at approximately 63%. This is not an accident — it is a reflection of the fact that REG content, while broad, is highly structured. The rules are rules. The math is math. There is very little ambiguity in what the AICPA wants you to know.

Contrast this with FAR, where the sheer volume of standards can feel overwhelming, or BEC (now discipline sections), where written communication adds unpredictability. In REG, if you know the rules, you get the points. Period.

Every hour you put in has a direct, measurable return. That is the REG deal. Take it.

### ANSWER KEY & EXPLANATIONS

**Q1 — B) $69,600**
Taylor's itemized deductions = $5,000 + $3,000 + $2,000 = $10,000. Since $10,000 < $14,600 standard deduction, use the standard deduction. Taxable income = $80,000 − $14,600 = $65,400. Wait — that's answer C. Let me reconsider: *Actually, answer C ($65,400) is correct.* Taylor uses the standard deduction ($14,600) because it exceeds itemized deductions ($10,000). Taxable income = $80,000 − $14,600 = **$65,400 (C)**.

✅ *Correct: C) $65,400.* Key rule: Always compare standard vs. itemized and take the larger amount.

**Q2 — C) Head of Household**
Jessica is unmarried, paid over 50% of household costs, and a qualifying child (age 14) lived with her all year. She qualifies for HOH status, which provides lower tax rates and a higher standard deduction than Single.

✅ *Correct: C) Head of Household.* Rule: HOH requires unmarried status, >50% of household costs, and a qualifying person living there >half the year.

**Q3 — B) $2,500**
AOTC = 100% of first $2,000 + 25% of next $2,000 = $2,000 + $500 = $2,500 maximum. Marcus paid $4,000, which covers the full maximum. His MAGI of $65,000 is well below the $80,000 phase-out floor for single filers.

✅ *Correct: B) $2,500.* Rule: AOTC max = $2,500 (100% × $2K + 25% × $2K). Phase-out for single: $80K–$90K.

**Q4 — B) Charitable contributions to a 501(c)(3)**
Charitable contributions are an ITEMIZED DEDUCTION (below-the-line). Student loan interest (A), HSA contributions (C), and 50% of SE tax (D) are all above-the-line adjustments.

✅ *Correct: B.* Rule: Charitable contributions are itemized deductions — they reduce taxable income only if the taxpayer itemizes.

**Q5 — A) $25,000**
Rivera's share of ordinary income = 30% × $50,000 = $15,000. Apply ACID-NL: Start $20,000 + $15,000 income = $35,000, then subtract $10,000 distribution = **$25,000 ending basis**.

✅ *Correct: A) $25,000.* Rule: ACID-NL order — increase for income BEFORE decreasing for distributions.

**Q6 — A) $40,000**
Recourse liabilities are shared according to the loss-sharing ratio for general partners. Partner A (general, 40% loss ratio) bears 40% × $100,000 = $40,000. Limited partners (Partner B) do NOT share in recourse liabilities.

✅ *Correct: A) $40,000.* Rule: Recourse liabilities → loss-sharing ratio (general partners). Nonrecourse liabilities → profit-sharing ratio (all partners).

**Q7 — B) $20,000 dividend income; $5,000 return of capital**
Dividend = lesser of distribution or current + accumulated E&P. Current E&P = $20,000 (positive); accumulated E&P = ($30,000) deficit. Use current E&P first: $20,000 is dividend income. Remaining $5,000 distribution: no E&P left, so return of capital (reduces basis).

✅ *Correct: B.* Rule: Current E&P is used first, even if there is an accumulated E&P deficit.

**Q8 — C) One of the shareholders is a C-corporation**
S-corps cannot have non-individual shareholders (with limited exceptions for certain trusts and estates). A C-corporation as a shareholder disqualifies the S election. Answer A: 75 shareholders is under the 100 limit — OK. Answer B: voting vs. nonvoting is permitted (only one class of stock economically). Answer D: age of the corporation is irrelevant.

✅ *Correct: C.* Rule: S-QUID — Qualified shareholders only (no corporations, no partnerships).

**Q9 — B) No gain or loss recognized**
Double-basis rule: Amy's gain basis = donor's basis ($40,000); Amy's loss basis = FMV at gift ($30,000). She sold for $35,000. Is there a gain? $35,000 < $40,000 gain basis — no gain. Is there a loss? $35,000 > $30,000 loss basis — no loss. Amy is in the "dead zone." No gain, no loss.

✅ *Correct: B.* Rule: Gift — sell between the FMV and donor's basis → neither gain nor loss recognized.

**Q10 — B) $20,000**
5-year MACRS, 200% declining balance rate = 200%/5 = 40% per year. Apply half-year convention: 40% × $100,000 × 1/2 = $20,000 in Year 1.

✅ *Correct: B) $20,000.* Rule: 200%DB rate = 2/recovery period. Half-year convention applies in Year 1.

**Q11 — B) $30,000 §1245 ordinary income**
Adjusted basis = $80,000 − $50,000 depreciation = $30,000. Realized gain = $60,000 − $30,000 = $30,000. §1245 recaptures gain to the extent of depreciation taken ($50,000). Since gain ($30,000) < depreciation ($50,000), ALL gain is §1245 ordinary income.

✅ *Correct: B) $30,000 §1245 ordinary income.* Rule: §1245 recaptures GAIN (not depreciation) as ordinary income — limited to lesser of gain or depreciation taken.

**Q12 — B) $15,000**
Realized gain = ($90,000 + $15,000) − $50,000 = $55,000. Recognized gain = lesser of realized gain ($55,000) or boot received ($15,000) = **$15,000**.

✅ *Correct: B) $15,000.* Rule: In §1031 exchanges, gain recognized = lesser of realized gain or boot received.

**Q13 — B) No — must be in writing**
The contract is for 2 years of lawn mowing services. It cannot be performed within one year from the date of the contract. The Statute of Frauds (MY LEGS — "Y" for Year) requires contracts that cannot be completed within one year to be in writing. This is a common law contract (services, not goods), so UCC Article 2 does not apply.

✅ *Correct: B.* Rule: MY LEGS "Y" — contracts that cannot be performed within one year from making must be in writing.

**Q14 — C) Both the agent and the principal, jointly and severally**
Under respondeat superior, a principal is vicariously liable for the torts of their agent committed within the scope of employment. However, the agent is also personally liable for their own negligence. Both are liable jointly and severally to the injured third party.

✅ *Correct: C.* Rule: Respondeat superior — principal AND agent are both liable for agent's torts in scope of employment.

**Q15 — D) When the seller completes delivery of conforming goods to the buyer**
Under UCC §2-401, absent specific agreement, title passes when the seller completes their performance with respect to physical delivery of the goods. If the contract requires delivery, title passes at delivery.

✅ *Correct: D.* Rule: UCC title passes on completion of seller's delivery obligation (conforming goods).

**Q16 — A) No — the parol evidence rule bars prior verbal agreements that contradict the written contract**
The parol evidence rule prevents the introduction of prior or contemporaneous oral agreements that would contradict or vary the terms of a fully integrated written contract. The written contract states $8,000; the verbal agreement at $7,500 contradicts it. The rule bars this evidence.

✅ *Correct: A.* Rule: Parol evidence rule — a fully integrated written contract cannot be contradicted by prior verbal agreements.

**Q17 — B) Greater of $1,000 or 50% of income derived from the return (§6694(a))**
A 15% probability does not meet the "substantial authority" standard (~40%). The position is not disclosed. The §6694(a) penalty for unreasonable positions = greater of $1,000 or 50% of fee income from the return.

✅ *Correct: B.* Rule: RSM ladder — below substantial authority without disclosure = §6694(a) penalty.

**Q18 — B) 3 years from the later of the return due date or filing date**
The standard assessment statute of limitations is 3 years from the later of: (1) the due date of the return (including extensions), or (2) the date the return was actually filed.

✅ *Correct: B.* Rule: 3-6-FOREVER — standard period = 3 years.

**Q19 — B) Yes — the 6-year statute applies**
The taxpayer omitted $60,000 from a return showing $200,000 of gross income. The omission percentage = $60,000/$200,000 = 30%, which exceeds 25%. The 6-year extended statute applies.

✅ *Correct: B.* Rule: 3-6-FOREVER — omission of >25% of gross income triggers the 6-year period.

**Q20 — B) Obtain written informed consent from all affected clients**
Under Circular 230, a CPA may represent conflicting interests only if: (1) the CPA reasonably believes they can represent all parties effectively, AND (2) all affected clients give informed written consent.

✅ *Correct: B.* Rule: Circular 230 — conflict of interest requires written informed consent from ALL affected clients.

## SECTION 7: ELITE-LEVEL FEEDBACK LOOPS
### Your Self-Improvement Operating System

Top scorers do not just study more — they study smarter. These seven feedback systems will make your study hours compound in value over time.

---

### System 1: Weekly Error Log

Create a simple log with these columns: Date | Question Type (MCQ/TBS) | Topic | Sub-topic | Error Type | Rule Violated | Corrected Rule

Every wrong answer gets logged. Every week, review the log and look for patterns:
- Are you consistently missing S-corp basis questions? You need more basis computation practice.
- Are you failing on contracts defenses? You need to drill the Statute of Frauds and parol evidence rule.
- Are your errors conceptual or computational? Different problems require different solutions.

The error log transforms random practice into deliberate improvement. Without it, you will make the same mistakes repeatedly without knowing why.

---

### System 2: The 3-Box System

This is a physical or digital flashcard system with three categories:

- **Box 1 (Don't Know):** New cards, and any card you got wrong
- **Box 2 (Shaky):** Cards you got right but needed to think hard about
- **Box 3 (Mastered):** Cards you can answer in under 3 seconds without hesitation

**The Rule:** Review Box 1 every day. Review Box 2 every 2–3 days. Review Box 3 weekly.

When you get a Box 3 card wrong, it moves BACK to Box 1. This keeps mastered material fresh and prevents the illusion of knowing.

Make cards for every mnemonic in this guide, every critical number, every computation formula, and every rule that you have gotten wrong at least once.

---

### System 3: Spaced Repetition Schedule

For any concept you learn, review it at these intervals: 1 day → 3 days → 7 days → 14 days → 30 days.

If you learn S-corp basis on Monday, review it Tuesday, Thursday, the following Monday, and two weeks later. This schedule exploits the spacing effect — each retrieval at the point of near-forgetting strengthens the memory trace more than reviewing when the information is fresh.

Many review courses (Becker, Anki integrations) offer built-in spaced repetition. If yours does not, set calendar reminders manually.

---

### System 4: The Teach-Back Method

After studying any topic, close your notes and explain the concept aloud — as if you are teaching a brand-new student who knows nothing about tax law.

If you stumble on any point, you have found a gap. Go back to your notes, resolve the gap, and try again.

This method is the most reliable indicator of true understanding. You can recognize correct answers on MCQs without understanding why they are correct. You cannot teach a concept without understanding it. For TBS performance (which requires active problem-solving, not recognition), teach-back is essential.

---

### System 5: TBS Autopsy

After every practice TBS simulation, complete a structured debrief:

1. **What was the tested concept?** (Name the specific rule or formula)
2. **What went right?** (Which parts did you compute correctly?)
3. **What went wrong?** (Which specific step failed — data identification, formula application, arithmetic, or interpretation?)
4. **What is the corrected procedure?** (Write out the correct step-by-step process)
5. **What would I do differently next time?** (Specific procedural change)

The TBS autopsy converts a missed simulation from a discouraging experience into a learning asset. Top scorers autopsy every simulation, even the ones they ace — there is always something to refine.

---

### System 6: Score Trending

Track your practice MCQ accuracy and full-length practice exam scores in a simple chart. Plot weekly. Draw a trend line.

**Benchmarks:**
- Weeks 1–2: 60–70% MCQ accuracy is normal
- Weeks 3–4: 70–78% is your target range
- Week 4+: 78–85% on full-length exams indicates exam readiness

If your trend line is flat or declining after Week 2, the problem is usually one of three things:
1. You are doing too many new MCQs and not reviewing wrong answers
2. You have a systematic conceptual gap (often basis or entity taxation)
3. Your practice schedule is inconsistent

Trending makes problems visible early. A declining trend in Week 2 is manageable. A declining trend in Week 4 requires aggressive intervention.

---

### System 7: The 0.1% Evaluation

After completing this full blueprint, conduct a final self-assessment using the AICPA's four skill levels:

- **Remember:** Can you recall the mnemonic or rule from memory in under 3 seconds?
- **Apply:** Can you execute the rule on a straightforward MCQ without looking it up?
- **Analyze:** Can you break down a multi-step TBS problem and identify which rules apply at each step?
- **Evaluate:** Can you handle conflicting rules, edge cases, and trap questions — and choose the best answer even when two options seem plausible?

The 0.1% evaluation question is simple: *Would the top 0.1% of REG scorers approve of my understanding of this concept?*

Be brutally honest. The exam will be. For every topic where the answer is "no," go back to the relevant section of this guide and the resources in Section 4 until the answer becomes "yes."

## SECTION 8: STORY-BASED METAPHORS FOR KEY CONCEPTS
### The Vivid Mental Models That Make Rules Permanent

The human brain remembers stories and images far better than rules and numbers. These metaphors are designed to create permanent mental anchors for the hardest REG concepts.

---

### Metaphor 1: The Tax Formula as a Waterfall

Your income begins at the top of a mountain waterfall — that is your gross income. Every dollar you earned from any source starts at the top.

As the water falls, it first passes through a set of rocks and boulders — those are your adjustments to income (above-the-line deductions). These rocks slow the water down significantly. What reaches the pool at the bottom is your AGI.

From the pool, the water must flow through a filtration system — either the standard filter (standard deduction) or a custom filter (itemized deductions). You choose whichever filter removes more water. What passes through is your taxable income.

Then the government brings a bucket. The size of the bucket is determined by the tax brackets — progressively larger buckets for more water. This is your gross tax liability.

But you have umbrellas. The tax credits are umbrellas held over the pool — they prevent the government from reaching certain water. Nonrefundable credits shield water from the government but don't create more water. Refundable credits actually add water back.

What's left after the umbrellas is what you pay — or what comes back to you as a refund.

Every time you see an individual tax question, play this waterfall in your mind. Where does this item enter the waterfall? Which rock is it? Which filter? Which umbrella?

---

### Metaphor 2: Basis as a Backpack

Every asset has an invisible backpack permanently strapped to it — that is its basis. The backpack's weight is what you paid for the asset on the day you acquired it.

When you buy something, the backpack weighs exactly what you paid (cost basis).

When someone gives it to you as a gift, the backpack transfers from them to you — same weight, same everything (carryover basis). But if the backpack was already lighter than what the asset is worth today (FMV < donor's basis), you get a special version: two weight markers, one for gains and one for losses (the double-basis rule).

When someone dies and leaves it to you, the backpack gets completely replaced. A brand-new backpack appears, and its weight is set to whatever the asset was worth on the day they died (step-up in basis). The old backpack — with all its accumulated history — is gone.

Depreciation is like the backpack getting lighter every year you use the asset for business. You are consuming its value, and the backpack reflects that.

When you sell, your gain is the difference between the sale price and how heavy the backpack currently is. If you sell for more than the backpack weighs, you have a gain. If you sell for less, you have a loss. The backpack's weight is everything.

---

### Metaphor 3: S-Corp Basis as a Bank Account

Your S-corp stock basis is a bank account that the IRS maintains for you.

When the S-corp earns income, the IRS deposits your share into your account (increases stock basis). When you take a distribution, you withdraw from the account (decreases stock basis). When the S-corp has a loss, the IRS charges a fee to your account (decreases stock basis).

The cardinal rule of this bank account: **You cannot overdraft.** Your balance cannot go below zero. If the fees (losses) exceed your balance, the fees get put in a holding queue — they wait until you make future deposits (future income) before they can be processed.

The ORDER in which transactions process matters — just as a real bank processes deposits before processing checks to prevent overdraft fees. In the S-corp bank account, income is processed before distributions are taken, and distributions are processed before losses are applied.

If you try to take a distribution that would overdraw your account — the excess is taxable. The bank does not allow it tax-free.

And remember: there is a second bank account for debt (debt basis). Loans you personally make to the corporation open a second account. But third-party loans you guaranteed do NOT open a debt basis account — only direct loans from you to the corporation count.

---

### Metaphor 4: The Statute of Frauds as "MY LEGS"

Picture yourself standing on a stage in front of a judge, arguing that a contract is enforceable. The judge looks down at you and says: "Can you stand on your own two feet — or do you have written proof to support you?"

You need written proof to keep your LEGS:

**M** — Marriage promises. You cannot just say you promised to marry someone in exchange for a gift. Put it in writing.

**Y** — Year-long contracts. If your deal cannot possibly be finished within one year from today, the law wants to see the ink.

**L** — Land deals. Real estate — buying, selling, leasing for more than a year — needs a signed writing.

**E** — Executor promises. If you personally promise to pay the deceased's debts out of your own pocket, write it down.

**G** — Goods over $500. Under the UCC, any sale of goods worth $500 or more needs a written contract.

**S** — Surety agreements. If you promise to pay someone else's debt if they default, the creditor needs that promise in writing.

Without written proof for any of these six, your legal LEGS collapse. The contract may exist, but you cannot enforce it in court.

---

### Metaphor 5: The Three Loss Filters as Airport Security

Your tax loss is a passenger trying to board a plane (your tax return) and deduct its fare (reduce your taxes). But there are three security checkpoints, and the loss must pass all three.

**Checkpoint 1 — Basis (Do You Have a Ticket?)**
The loss can only board if you have enough basis in the investment. Basis is your ticket. No basis, no boarding. The loss must wait in the terminal (carried forward).

**Checkpoint 2 — At-Risk (Is Your Luggage Within Weight Limits?)**
Even with a ticket, your loss can only board up to the amount you have truly at economic risk — cash invested, and amounts borrowed for which you are personally liable. Recourse debt adds to at-risk; nonrecourse debt (generally) does not. Overweight luggage (losses beyond your at-risk amount) is checked and held — it boards on a later flight when you put more skin in the game.

**Checkpoint 3 — Passive Activity (Do You Have the Right Boarding Pass?)**
Even if your loss has a ticket and properly weighted luggage, it needs the right boarding pass. Passive losses can only offset passive income. If you are a passive investor (don't materially participate), your loss is redirected to the passive lounge — it waits there until you have passive income to offset, or until you fully dispose of the activity.

Every loss deduction you claim on your tax return has cleared all three checkpoints. Every suspended loss is sitting in one of the three waiting areas.

## SECTION 10: 5 CONCEPT-BUILDING FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS
### Your Current Level Assessment

These five questions are not trivia — they are diagnostic instruments. Answer each one carefully. What you can and cannot answer tells you exactly where to focus next.

---

### Question 1: The Tax Formula from Memory

**The Challenge:** Write out the complete individual tax formula from Gross Income to Net Tax Owed/Refund from memory — no notes, no looking at Section 1.

**What a correct answer looks like:**
Gross Income → minus Adjustments (above-the-line deductions) = AGI → minus greater of Standard or Itemized Deductions → minus QBI deduction = Taxable Income → times Tax Rate = Gross Tax Liability → minus Credits (nonrefundable first, then refundable) = Net Tax Liability → minus Payments (withholding, estimated tax) = Amount Owed or Refund Due.

**What this reveals:** If you can write the entire formula without pausing, you have the mental architecture for all individual tax questions. Every question maps to one step on this formula — and knowing where to map it eliminates wrong answers instantly.

**What a wrong answer reveals:** Most students who struggle miss either (1) the distinction between above-the-line and below-the-line deductions, (2) the sequencing of credits vs. payments, or (3) the QBI deduction step. If you stumble on any of these, go back to Days 1–6 of the roadmap and the waterfall metaphor in Section 8.

---

### Question 2: S-Corp Basis Calculation

**The Challenge:** An S-corp has a 40% shareholder. At the start of the year, the shareholder's stock basis is $15,000. During the year: the S-corp reports $100,000 of ordinary income and $8,000 of separately stated charitable contributions. A $30,000 distribution is made to the shareholder. Calculate the shareholder's ending stock basis.

**What a correct answer looks like:**
- Start: $15,000
- Add 40% × $100,000 ordinary income: +$40,000 → $55,000
- Decrease for $30,000 distribution (40% × $30,000): −$12,000 → $43,000
- Decrease for 40% × $8,000 charitable contributions: −$3,200 → **$39,800 ending basis**

**What this reveals:** Mastery of the ACID-NL ordering rule, the ability to apply percentages to per-share items, and the discipline to process distributions before losses (as required).

**What a wrong answer reveals:** Common errors include (1) applying losses before distributions, (2) forgetting that charitable contributions reduce basis even though they are separately stated, or (3) confusing the shareholder's share percentage. Review Section 1, Topic 3 and work through 10 more basis computation problems.

---

### Question 3: The §1231/1245/1250 Hierarchy

**The Challenge:** Explain — without looking at notes — the difference between Section 1231, Section 1245, and Section 1250. Then describe the ORDER in which you apply these rules to a gain on the sale of a business asset.

**What a correct answer looks like:**
§1245 is applied first — it recaptures all depreciation taken on personal property as ordinary income, but only to the extent of gain. §1250 is applied next — for real property, it recaptures only "additional depreciation" (MACRS depreciation over straight-line) as ordinary income. Under current law, most §1250 recapture is zero because MACRS already uses straight-line for real property (though "unrecaptured §1250 gain" is taxed at a maximum 25% rate). Any remaining gain after recapture is §1231 gain — taxed at long-term capital gain rates if net §1231 is positive, or ordinary loss rates if net §1231 is negative. And the §1231 lookback rule can recharacterize current gains as ordinary if prior-year §1231 losses were taken.

**What this reveals:** You understand not just the definitions but the interaction between these provisions — which is what TBS questions require.

**What a wrong answer reveals:** Most students who fail this question either (1) cannot remember the correct order (§1245 before §1250 before §1231), or (2) don't understand that §1231 is the final net calculation, not an initial filter. Review Section 1, Topic 8, and the "recapture order" mnemonic. Work through three complete gain/loss computation problems.

---

### Question 4: Statute of Frauds and Contract Enforceability

**The Challenge:** A seller and buyer orally agree to the sale of a house for $500,000. The buyer makes a 10% down payment and moves in. Later, the seller claims the contract is unenforceable because it was oral. Who is correct, and what is the legal rule at stake?

**What a correct answer looks like:**
The Statute of Frauds (MY LEGS — "L" for Land) requires contracts for the sale of real estate to be in writing to be enforceable. Ordinarily, this oral contract would be unenforceable. However, there is an important exception: **part performance** can remove a contract for land from the Statute of Frauds. The buyer has made a down payment AND taken possession — two acts of part performance. Most courts would hold the contract enforceable despite the lack of writing because part performance demonstrates the existence of the contract and it would be inequitable to allow the seller to escape it.

**What this reveals:** You understand not just the basic rule (MY LEGS) but its exceptions — which is where exam points are actually awarded.

**What a wrong answer reveals:** If you said "unenforceable" without mentioning part performance, you know the rule but not its exceptions. Review Section 1, Topic 9 and the exceptions to the Statute of Frauds. The exam frequently tests these edge cases.

---

### Question 5: Preparer Penalty and Standards

**The Challenge:** A tax preparer takes a return position that the preparer believes has a "reasonable basis" (approximately 15–20% likelihood of being sustained). The position is NOT disclosed on the return. What penalty applies to the preparer, and what is the dollar amount?

**What a correct answer looks like:**
The position does not meet the "substantial authority" standard (approximately 40% likelihood). It is also not disclosed. Under §6694(a), the preparer is subject to a penalty equal to the greater of $1,000 or 50% of the income the preparer derived from the return. This is the RSM "R" level — reasonable basis without disclosure does NOT protect the preparer from the §6694(a) penalty. The preparer would need substantial authority (no disclosure required) or at least reasonable basis WITH disclosure to avoid this penalty.

**What this reveals:** You understand the three-level RSM standards ladder, the interaction between disclosure and penalty avoidance, and the specific dollar amounts.

**What a wrong answer reveals:** If you confused §6694(a) and §6694(b), you are conflating the "unreasonable position" penalty with the "willful/reckless" penalty. If you missed the disclosure piece, you need to revisit how disclosure affects penalty exposure. Review Section 1, Topic 11 and the RSM mnemonic completely.

### The Framework That Separates Passers from Elite Scorers

### Bloom's Taxonomy Applied to REG

The AICPA explicitly designs REG questions to test different cognitive skill levels. Understanding which level a question is testing tells you how to approach it.

**Level 1 — Remember:** Can you recall the mnemonic, rule, or number from memory in under 3 seconds?
- *Example:* "What does MY LEGS stand for?" or "What is the standard deduction for a single filer in 2024?"
- *This level covers:* ~15% of MCQs. These are straightforward recall questions. You either know it or you don't. Drilling flashcards addresses this level.

**Level 2 — Understand:** Can you explain WHY the rule exists in plain language?
- *Example:* "Why does the gift tax double-basis rule exist?" (Answer: to prevent taxpayers from transferring losses between parties by gifting depreciated property)
- *This level covers:* ~25% of MCQs. Understanding the purpose of a rule helps you apply it correctly even when the question presents a novel fact pattern.

**Level 3 — Apply:** Can you use the rule correctly in a straightforward new scenario?
- *Example:* Given specific facts, calculate the shareholder's ending S-corp stock basis.
- *This level covers:* ~40% of MCQs and most TBS questions. This is the core of REG competency. Developing this skill requires working through computation problems, not just reading rules.

**Level 4 — Analyze:** Can you break down a complex, multi-step problem and identify which rules govern each component?
- *Example:* A problem involving a §1031 exchange with boot, where the replacement property is then depreciated, and a partial loss is later recognized — which depreciation rules apply? What is the basis of the replacement property? Is the loss recognized?
- *This level covers:* ~15% of MCQs and the most challenging TBS questions. Developing this skill requires working through integrated case studies.

**Level 5 — Evaluate:** Can you determine the best approach when multiple rules are in tension, or when two answer choices both seem correct?
- *Example:* "Which of the following would result in the LOWEST tax liability for the taxpayer?" (Requires evaluating multiple strategies against each other)
- *This level covers:* ~5% of MCQs but is often the difference between a 75 and an 85+. This is the hardest skill to develop and requires exposure to high-difficulty practice questions and full-length exam simulations.

**Your 0.1% Benchmark:** For each of the 12 high-yield topics in Section 1, you should be operating at Level 3 (Apply) at minimum, and Level 4 (Analyze) for entity basis calculations and property transactions.

### The "Inversion" Challenge: Reframe Your Assumptions

The most powerful thinking tool in this guide is inversion. Instead of asking "How do I pass REG?", ask "What would definitely make me fail REG?" — then systematically eliminate those failure patterns.

**What definitely fails candidates:**

**Failure Pattern 1: MCQ-Only Practice**
Candidates who exclusively practice MCQs and ignore TBS simulations consistently fail. TBS questions represent a significant portion of your score. They require not just knowing rules but applying them in integrated, multi-step scenarios. If you have not completed at least 15 full TBS simulations before exam day, you are undercooked.
*Fix: Start TBS practice by Day 6 of the roadmap. Do at least one TBS simulation every weekend.*

**Failure Pattern 2: Memorizing Without Understanding**
A candidate who memorizes "ACID-NL" but cannot execute a basis computation under exam pressure has an illusion of knowledge. The exam tests application, not recitation.
*Fix: For every mnemonic you learn, immediately work through three computation problems using that rule.*

**Failure Pattern 3: Skipping Basis Calculations**
Basis appears in individual taxation, property transactions, S-corps, and partnerships. Candidates who avoid it because "the math is hard" are conceding 30–40% of the exam.
*Fix: Commit to 20 basis computation problems per week throughout your study period. Make basis your comfort zone, not your avoidance zone.*

**Failure Pattern 4: Cramming Business Law Last-Minute**
Business law represents 15–25% of the exam. Candidates who leave it for the last few days cannot absorb the contract formation rules, UCC distinctions, agency principles, and business structure comparisons in time.
*Fix: Begin business law in Week 3 (as the roadmap specifies), not Week 4.*

**Failure Pattern 5: Ignoring the Error Log**
Candidates who do MCQs, check answers, feel frustrated about wrong ones, and move on — without logging the errors and reviewing the pattern — will repeat the same mistakes on exam day.
*Fix: Start your error log on Day 1. Review it every Sunday.*

---

**What you can safely skip (low-yield):**

REG is broad. You cannot master everything in 140 hours. These areas have historically low exam representation and can be reviewed lightly rather than deeply studied:

- Obscure penalty structures (e.g., specific dollar amounts for information return penalties)
- Rare entity types (personal holding companies, real estate mortgage investment conduits)
- Niche tax credits beyond the ones listed in Section 1
- Detailed state tax law (the exam tests federal law only, with minimal state reference)
- Specific UCC Article 9 secured transactions provisions beyond the basics

Use the time you save by deprioritizing these areas to go deeper on the 12 high-yield topics. Depth on the right topics beats breadth on the wrong ones every time.
