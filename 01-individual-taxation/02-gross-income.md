# 02 — Gross Income

## What Is Gross Income?

**The Legal Definition (IRC § 61):** Gross income means **all income from whatever source derived**, unless specifically excluded by law.

✅ Think of it like a giant bucket. The government says: "Everything that falls into this bucket counts as income — money you earn, money you win, money someone gives you for work, stuff you find. UNLESS there's a specific rule that says we don't count it." The exclusions are the exceptions; gross income is the default rule.

---

## The "All Income from Whatever Source Derived" Rule

This is the **broadest possible definition** of income. The IRS presumes ALL economic accessions to wealth are taxable. The burden is on the **taxpayer** to show an exclusion applies.

Key principles:
- Income includes money, property, services, and anything of value received
- It does not matter if payment is in cash, kind, or another form
- Illegal income is still includable (yes, really)
- Claim of right doctrine: if you receive money and have an unrestricted right to it, it's income even if you might have to return it later
- **Constructive receipt:** Income is taxable when it is made available to you, not just when you actually receive it (see below)

## The 14 Items of Gross Income — Detailed

| # | Income Item | Key Rules |
|---|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | **Wages & Salaries** | All compensation for services: salary, bonuses, tips, commissions, vacation pay |
| 2 | **Interest Income** | Taxable for bank accounts, corporate bonds, federal bonds. Municipal bonds are excluded. |
| 3 | **Dividends** | Ordinary dividends (taxed as ordinary income) vs. qualified dividends (0%/15%/20% rates) |
| 4 | **Alimony Received (pre-2019 agreements)** | Divorce decrees executed before 1/1/2019: alimony is income to recipient. Post-2018 agreements: NOT included (and NOT deductible by payor) |
| 5 | **Business Income** | Net profit from sole proprietorship (Schedule C); gross revenue minus business expenses |
| 6 | **Capital Gains** | Gains from sale of capital assets (stocks, bonds, real estate held for investment) |
| 7 | **IRA Distributions** | Traditional IRA distributions fully taxable; Roth distributions generally tax-free if qualified |
| 8 | **Pensions/Annuities** | Taxable to the extent payments exceed the taxpayer's after-tax investment (cost basis) |
| 9 | **Rental Income** | Gross rents received; deduct expenses on Schedule E to get net rental income/loss |
| 10 | **Royalties** | Payments for use of property (oil/gas, copyrights, patents); reported on Schedule E |
| 11 | **K-1 Flow-Through** | Partnership, S corp, trust income passed through to individual taxpayer via Schedule K-1 |
| 12 | **Unemployment Compensation** | Fully included in gross income (100%) |
| 13 | **Social Security Benefits** | Up to 85% included depending on "combined income" (AGI + nontaxable interest + 50% SS) |
| 14 | **State/Local Tax Refunds** | Included ONLY to the extent the taxpayer received a tax benefit from deducting the original state taxes (tax benefit rule) |

## Social Security Income Inclusion Formula

| Combined Income | Married Filing Jointly | Single |
|-----------------|----------------------|--------|
| Below base amount | 0% included | 0% included |
| $32,001 – $44,000 | Up to 50% included | $25,001 – $34,000: up to 50% |
| Over $44,000 | Up to 85% included | Over $34,000: up to 85% |

✅ Think of it this way: Social Security benefits are "partially taxed." The more other income you have, the more of your Social Security the government taxes. The maximum they can ever tax is 85 cents out of every dollar of SS benefits.

## Constructive Receipt Doctrine

**Rule:** Income is taxable in the year it is **made available** to the taxpayer, even if not actually received.

✅ Imagine your boss writes you a check on December 28 and puts it on your desk. Even if you don't pick it up until January 2, you still have December income because the money was available to you.

**Key applications:**
- Check received in December → income in December even if not cashed until January
- Interest credited to your bank account → income when credited, not when withdrawn
- Salary check placed in mailbox → income when placed, not when opened

**Limits to constructive receipt:**
- Cannot receive income constructively if there are substantial limitations or restrictions on it
- Employee cannot delay income by asking employer to hold checks

## Exclusions from Gross Income

These items are **NOT included** in gross income (they are specifically excluded by statute):

| Exclusion | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Gifts** | Fair market value of gifts excluded from gross income (but donor may owe gift tax). Must be a genuine gift — not compensation for services. |
| **Inheritances** | Property inherited is excluded from gross income. (But income earned by inherited property after receipt is taxable.) |
| **Life Insurance Proceeds** | Death benefit paid to beneficiary excluded. Exception: if policy was transferred for valuable consideration (transfer for value rule). |
| **Municipal Bond Interest** | Interest on state and local government bonds excluded from federal gross income. |
| **Scholarships** | Amounts used for tuition, fees, books, and supplies excluded. Room, board, and stipends are taxable. Recipient must be a degree candidate. |
| **Employer-Provided Benefits** | Health insurance premiums, up to $5,850/$12,100 (2024) in employer HSA contributions, up to $5,250 in employer educational assistance, up to $300/month in parking/transit benefits. |
| **Child Support Received** | Never included in gross income (contrast with pre-2019 alimony which is included). |
| **Workers' Compensation** | Amounts received for job-related injuries/illness excluded. |
| **Qualified Foster Care Payments** | Payments from state for foster care excluded. |
| **Combat Pay** | Enlisted military members can exclude combat pay. |
| **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion** | Up to $126,500 (2024) for US citizens living and working abroad (Form 2555). |

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** The exam loves the **tax benefit rule** for state tax refunds. If a taxpayer took the standard deduction (and got no benefit from paying state taxes), the refund is NOT included in gross income.

> **Tip 2:** **Scholarships** — memorize what's in vs. out. Tuition and required fees/books = excluded. Room and board = TAXABLE.

> **Tip 3:** **Life insurance proceeds** are excluded — unless the policy was transferred for value. Know the transfer-for-value exception.

> **Tip 4:** The exam frequently tests **qualified vs. ordinary dividends**. Qualified dividends get preferential rates (0%/15%/20%). Ordinary dividends are taxed at ordinary income rates.

> **Tip 5:** **Constructive receipt** is a classic MCQ trap. If a check was available before December 31, it's income in that year regardless of when cashed.

> **Tip 6:** The "all income from whatever source derived" default rule means when in doubt, **include** it. Look for a specific exclusion to take it out.
