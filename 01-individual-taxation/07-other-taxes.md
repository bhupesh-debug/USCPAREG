# 07 — Other Taxes

## Overview

Beyond regular income tax, individuals may owe several additional taxes. These are the "other taxes" that appear on Schedule 2 of Form 1040.

✅ Think of these as extra charges tacked onto your regular tax bill for specific activities — running your own business, earning too much investment income, or not paying your taxes throughout the year.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "SANE" — The Four Other Individual Taxes

> **S**elf-employment tax  
> **A**dditional Medicare tax (0.9%)  
> **N**et Investment Income Tax (3.8% NIIT)  
> **E**stimated tax payments and underpayment penalties  

---

## 1. Self-Employment (SE) Tax

### What Is It?

When you work as an employee, your employer pays **half** of your Social Security and Medicare taxes. When you're self-employed, **you pay both halves**. That's the SE tax.

✅ As an employee, your employer quietly takes FICA taxes from your paycheck AND pays an equal amount themselves — you only see half the cost. As a self-employed person, there's no employer — so you're paying both the employee's share AND the employer's share yourself.

### The Rate Breakdown

| Tax Component | Rate | Wage Base (2024) |
|--------------|------|-----------------|
| Social Security | 12.4% | First $168,600 |
| Medicare | 2.9% | All SE income |
| **Total SE Tax** | **15.3%** | SS portion limited; Medicare unlimited |

### How SE Tax Is Calculated

```
Step 1: Net SE income
Step 2: × 92.35% (= 100% − 7.65%) → "Net earnings from SE"
Step 3: × 15.3% = SE Tax
Step 4: SE Tax ÷ 2 = Deductible portion (above-the-line deduction)
```

✅ Why 92.35%? Because employers get to deduct the employer's half of FICA from their own income. To give self-employed people the same benefit, you only pay SE tax on 92.35% of your income, not the full 100%.

**Example:** Net SE income = $100,000
1. $100,000 × 92.35% = $92,350
2. $92,350 × 15.3% = $14,130 SE tax
3. $14,130 ÷ 2 = $7,065 deductible as adjustment to income

### Additional Medicare Tax for SE Individuals

SE individuals also owe the **0.9% Additional Medicare Tax** on SE income above:
- $200,000 (single/HOH)
- $250,000 (MFJ)
- $125,000 (MFS)

## 2. Additional Medicare Tax (0.9%)

**Rate:** 0.9% on wages/compensation/SE income exceeding thresholds.

| Filing Status | Threshold |
|--------------|-----------|
| Single / HOH / Qualifying Widow(er) | **$200,000** |
| Married Filing Jointly | **$250,000** |
| Married Filing Separately | **$125,000** |

✅ Once you earn enough, the government takes an extra 0.9 cents out of every dollar you earn above the threshold. This is on top of the regular 2.9% Medicare tax.

**Key points:**
- Employers withhold the extra 0.9% once employee wages exceed $200,000 in a year (from that employer), **regardless** of filing status
- If a married couple's combined wages trigger the tax, they reconcile on their joint return — they may owe more or get a credit for overwithholding
- SE individuals include the 0.9% in their SE tax calculation on Schedule SE

---

## 3. Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) — 3.8%

**Rate:** **3.8%** on the lesser of (1) net investment income, or (2) excess of MAGI over the threshold.

| Filing Status | Threshold |
|--------------|-----------|
| Single / HOH | **$200,000** |
| Married Filing Jointly | **$250,000** |
| Married Filing Separately | **$125,000** |

### What Is "Net Investment Income"?

| **Included (NII)** | **Excluded (NOT NII)** |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| Interest | Wages and salaries |
| Dividends | Self-employment income |
| Annuities | Retirement distributions (IRA, 401k) |
| Royalties | Active trade/business income |
| Rents (passive) | Social Security benefits |
| Capital gains (most) | Alimony |
| Passive activity income | Tax-exempt interest |

✅ The NIIT is a "surtax" on investment income for higher-income individuals. If you're rich and live off investments, you pay an extra 3.8% on that passive income. Working people earning wages don't pay this tax.

**Example Calculation:**
- MAGI: $300,000
- Threshold (MFJ): $250,000
- Excess of MAGI over threshold: $50,000
- Net Investment Income: $80,000
- Lesser of $50,000 and $80,000 = **$50,000**
- NIIT = $50,000 × 3.8% = **$1,900**

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "3.8 on the Gate" — NIIT Threshold Reminder

> The gate opens at $200K (single) or $250K (MFJ). Once you cross the gate, you pay **3.8%** on your investment income. Below the gate? No NIIT.

## 4. Estimated Tax Payments

### Who Must Pay?

Taxpayers whose withholding doesn't cover their tax liability must make **quarterly estimated tax payments** — typically:
- Self-employed individuals
- Investors with large capital gains
- Anyone who expects to owe $1,000+ more than withholding

✅ Employees have taxes taken out of each paycheck automatically. Self-employed people and investors don't have an employer to do this — so the government says: "Pay us a little bit every quarter, or we'll charge you a penalty at year-end."

### Quarterly Due Dates

| Payment Period | Due Date |
|---------------|---------|
| January 1 – March 31 | **April 15** |
| April 1 – May 31 | **June 15** |
| June 1 – August 31 | **September 15** |
| September 1 – December 31 | **January 15** (following year) |

✅ The payment schedule is **not** evenly quarterly — notice the second quarter only covers 2 months (April–May), while the fourth quarter covers 4 months (September–December).

---

## Safe Harbor Rules — Avoiding Underpayment Penalties

To avoid an underpayment penalty, you must pay the **lesser of**:

| Safe Harbor | Rule |
|------------|------|
| **Current Year Safe Harbor** | Pay at least **90%** of current year's tax liability |
| **Prior Year Safe Harbor** | Pay **100% of prior year's tax** (110% if prior year AGI > $150,000) |

✅ There are two ways to stay safe: (1) Pay almost all of this year's taxes (90%), or (2) Pay exactly what you paid last year (100%, or 110% if you were high-income). You only need to satisfy ONE of these two tests.

**The 110% rule:** If your prior-year AGI exceeded $150,000 (or $75,000 MFS), you must pay **110%** of prior year's tax as a safe harbor (not just 100%).

---

## Underpayment Penalty

If you don't meet a safe harbor:
- **Penalty rate:** Calculated on the underpayment × the federal short-term rate + 3%
- Computed separately for each quarter (based on what you should have paid by each due date)
- Filed on **Form 2210**
- Can be waived if underpayment is due to casualty/disaster or retirement/disability

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "90-100-110" — Safe Harbor Numbers

> **90%** of current year's tax, OR  
> **100%** of prior year's tax (if prior AGI ≤ $150,000), OR  
> **110%** of prior year's tax (if prior AGI > $150,000)  
> You need to hit just ONE of these to avoid the penalty.

---

## Quick Comparison: Additional Medicare vs. NIIT

| Feature | Additional Medicare Tax | Net Investment Income Tax |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Rate | 0.9% | 3.8% |
| Applies to | Wages, SE income | Investment income only |
| Threshold (Single) | $200,000 | $200,000 |
| Threshold (MFJ) | $250,000 | $250,000 |
| Applies to wage earners? | Yes | No |
| Applies to SE income? | Yes | No |
| Applies to capital gains? | No | Yes |
| Applies to dividends? | No | Yes |

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** SE tax = **15.3%** (12.4% SS + 2.9% Medicare) on **92.35%** of net SE income. Know both numbers — the exam tests both the rate and the income base.

> **Tip 2:** The **deductible portion of SE tax** (50%) is an **above-the-line deduction** — it reduces AGI. This is different from a Schedule C expense.

> **Tip 3:** **NIIT does NOT apply to IRA/retirement distributions** — a very common exam trap. Retirement distributions are excluded from NII even if paid to a high-income taxpayer.

> **Tip 4:** The safe harbor **110% rule** kicks in when prior-year AGI exceeds $150,000. This is a frequently tested threshold.

> **Tip 5:** Estimated tax due dates are **not quarterly** — the second "quarter" covers only 2 months. Know the actual due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15.

> **Tip 6:** The 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax and the 3.8% NIIT have the **same income thresholds** ($200K single, $250K MFJ) but they apply to **different types of income**.
