# 05 — Section 199A Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction

## What Is the QBI Deduction?

The **Section 199A deduction** allows eligible self-employed individuals and pass-through business owners to deduct up to **20% of qualified business income (QBI)** from their taxable income.

✅ Imagine you own a small business and made $100,000 in profit. The government says: "We'll let you pretend you only made $80,000 of that as taxable income — you get a 20% discount on your business profits." That's the QBI deduction. It was created by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) to give pass-through business owners a tax break similar to the lower corporate tax rate.

**It reduces taxable income** — not AGI. It's a deduction taken **after** AGI is calculated.

---

## Who Qualifies for the QBI Deduction?

Eligible taxpayers include:
- **Sole proprietors** (Schedule C filers)
- **Partners** in a partnership (Schedule K-1 income)
- **S corporation shareholders** (Schedule K-1 income)
- **Beneficiaries** of trusts and estates (certain)
- **REIT dividend recipients** (20% of qualified REIT dividends)
- **Qualified cooperative dividends**

**Does NOT apply to:**
- C corporations (they have the flat 21% rate instead)
- W-2 employees
- Investment income (capital gains, dividends, interest)

---

## The Basic Calculation

```
QBI Deduction = Lesser of:
  (1) 20% of QBI from each qualifying trade or business, OR
  (2) 20% of (Taxable Income - Net Capital Gains)
```

✅ You get 20% of your business income, BUT you can't get more than 20% of your total taxable income (after subtracting capital gains). The second limit prevents very high earners from using QBI to reduce taxes below the capital gains rate.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "QBI = Quarter of Business Income" (It's 20% — Close Enough!)

> Remember: **One-Fifth** (20%) of your qualifying business income. Think "a fifth of your earnings goes back to you tax-free." It's not quite a quarter (25%), but "quarter" is easier to remember, and you'll correct yourself to 20% on the exam.

---

## Specified Service Trades or Businesses (SSTBs)

**SSTBs are disfavored** — the deduction phases out and eventually disappears for SSTB owners with high income.

**SSTB industries include:**
- Health (physicians, dentists, nurses)
- Law
- Accounting
- Actuarial science
- Performing arts
- Consulting
- Athletics
- Financial services (brokers, investment advisors)
- Brokerage services
- Any trade where the principal asset is the **reputation or skill** of the owner

**Does NOT include:**
- Engineering
- Architecture
- Real estate
- Insurance

✅ A doctor's medical practice = SSTB. A real estate company = NOT an SSTB. An engineering firm = NOT an SSTB. The rule basically says: "If your business is just YOU (your skills), it's a SSTB."

---

## Income Thresholds and Phase-Out Rules (2024)

| Filing Status | Phase-Out Begins | Phase-Out Ends (SSTB fully excluded) |
|--------------|-----------------|--------------------------------------|
| Single/MFS/HOH | $191,950 | $241,950 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $383,900 | $483,900 |

**What the thresholds mean:**

| Taxable Income Level | Non-SSTB | SSTB |
|---------------------|---------|------|
| Below lower threshold | **Full 20% deduction** | **Full 20% deduction** |
| In phase-out range | Subject to W-2 wage limit (phase-in) | Phase-out applies; partial deduction |
| Above upper threshold | Subject to full W-2 wage limit | **No QBI deduction** |

---

## W-2 Wage Limitation

For taxpayers **above the lower threshold**, the deduction is limited to the **greater of**:

1. **50% of W-2 wages** paid by the business, OR
2. **25% of W-2 wages** + **2.5% of unadjusted basis** of qualified property

✅ This limit encourages businesses to hire employees and invest in property. A business with no employees and no property might get a very small QBI deduction once the owner's income exceeds the threshold.

**Below the lower threshold:** The W-2 wage limitation does NOT apply — you get the full 20% deduction without any wage testing.

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: "SSTB = Sorry, Service Taxpayer, Bye-Bye" (High Income = No QBI)

> Once your taxable income exceeds the upper threshold, SSTB owners get **zero** QBI deduction. Non-SSTB businesses still get it (subject to wage limits). Remember: Engineers and architects are **not** SSTBs — they're favored under the law.

---

## Phase-In of W-2 Wage Limitation

For taxpayers in the **phase-out range** (between lower and upper threshold):

```
W-2 Wage Limitation Phase-In:

Deduction = 20% of QBI − [Phase-in %  × (20% of QBI − W-2 Wage Limit)]

Where Phase-in % = (Taxable Income − Lower Threshold) / $100,000 (MFJ) or $50,000 (Single)
```

✅ Think of it as a sliding scale: right at the lower threshold, you get the full deduction. As your income rises toward the upper threshold, more and more of the wage limitation kicks in. Once you hit the upper threshold, you're fully subject to the W-2 wage limit.

---

## REIT Dividends and Qualified PTP Income

- **Qualified REIT dividends:** Eligible for 20% deduction regardless of W-2 wages and regardless of whether taxpayer is above the threshold
- **Qualified publicly traded partnership (PTP) income:** Also eligible for 20% deduction
- Both are added to QBI from pass-through businesses before applying the 20%

---

## Summary: QBI Deduction Decision Tree

```
Is taxpayer below the lower threshold?
  → YES: Take 20% of QBI (no W-2 wage test, no SSTB restriction)
  → NO: Continue...

Is the business an SSTB?
  → YES: Is income above the upper threshold?
       → YES: No QBI deduction
       → NO: Apply phase-out reduction
  → NO: Apply W-2 wage/property limitation

Final deduction = Lesser of:
  (1) 20% of QBI (adjusted for any phase-outs)
  (2) 20% of (Taxable Income − Net Capital Gains)
```

---

## 🧠 Mnemonic: QBI Limits in Order — "WTWP"

> **W**ho qualifies (pass-through only)  
> **T**axable income threshold (below = easy, above = harder)  
> **W**ages/property limitation (applies above threshold)  
> **P**hase-out for SSTBs (disappears at upper threshold)

## Exam Tips

> **Tip 1:** The exam frequently tests whether a business IS or IS NOT an SSTB. Remember: **Engineering and architecture are NOT SSTBs** — they were specifically excluded from the SSTB definition.

> **Tip 2:** The QBI deduction does NOT reduce AGI — it reduces **taxable income**. Don't use it as an above-the-line adjustment.

> **Tip 3:** Below the lower threshold = **simple**. No W-2 wage limit, no SSTB restriction. Answer is always 20% of QBI (capped at 20% of taxable income).

> **Tip 4:** The QBI deduction is **not available to C corporations**. It's specifically designed for pass-through entities — sole props, partnerships, S corps.

> **Tip 5:** Rental income may qualify for QBI if the rental activity rises to the level of a "trade or business" — this is often tested. A single rental property with minimal management activity generally does not qualify.

> **Tip 6:** REIT dividends qualify for the 20% deduction even if above thresholds, without a W-2 wage test.
